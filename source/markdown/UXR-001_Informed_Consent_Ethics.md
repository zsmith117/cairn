## 1. Purpose

This Standard Operating Procedure establishes the requirements and processes for obtaining informed consent from research participants and conducting UX research in an ethical manner. It ensures that:

- Participants voluntarily agree to participate with full understanding of what involvement entails
- Participant rights, privacy, and dignity are protected throughout the research process
- The organization minimizes legal and reputational risk
- Research data is collected, stored, and used appropriately
- The team maintains consistent ethical standards across all studies

This SOP applies to all UX research activities regardless of method, location, or participant type.

---

## 2. Scope

### 2.1 This SOP Applies To:

- All UX research studies conducted by or on behalf of the organization
- All research methods including interviews, usability tests, surveys, field studies, diary studies, focus groups, and observational research
- Research conducted in-person, remotely, or via unmoderated tools
- Internal employees participating as research subjects
- External participants including customers, prospects, partners, and end users
- Research conducted at company facilities, participant workplaces, or third-party locations

### 2.2 This SOP Does Not Apply To:

- Customer feedback collected through standard support channels (unless used for formal research)
- Analytics data collected under existing website/product privacy policies
- Competitive analysis using publicly available information
- Sales or marketing conversations not conducted for research purposes

### 2.3 Special Considerations Covered:

- B2B research with enterprise customers
- Research in industrial or manufacturing environments
- Studies involving proprietary or confidential business information
- Multi-stakeholder research within client organizations
- International research with varying legal requirements

---

## 3. Definitions

**Informed Consent:** A process by which a participant voluntarily confirms their willingness to participate in research after being informed of all aspects relevant to their decision. Consent must be given freely, without coercion, and can be withdrawn at any time.

**Participant:** Any individual who provides data, opinions, behaviors, or other information as part of a UX research study. This includes users, customers, employees, and other stakeholders.

**Personally Identifiable Information (PII):** Any data that could potentially identify a specific individual, including name, email, phone number, job title combined with company name, photographs, video recordings, voice recordings, or IP addresses.

**Sensitive Information:** Data requiring additional protection, including health information, financial details, employment records, authentication credentials, or proprietary business information.

**Research Sponsor:** The internal stakeholder or team requesting and funding the research study.

**Data Controller:** The party responsible for determining the purposes and means of processing personal data (typically the organization conducting the research).

**Anonymous Data:** Data that cannot be linked back to an individual participant, even by the research team.

**Confidential Data:** Data that can be linked to participants by the research team but is not disclosed externally in identifiable form.

**NDA (Non-Disclosure Agreement):** A legal contract establishing confidentiality obligations between parties, often required when research involves proprietary information.

## 4. Procedure

### 4.1 Pre-Study Planning

#### 4.1.1 Determine Consent Requirements

Before recruiting participants, the researcher must assess:

| Factor | Consideration |
|--------|---------------|
| Recording | Will sessions be audio/video recorded? |
| Data Sensitivity | Will you collect PII or sensitive business information? |
| Observation | Will others observe sessions (live or recorded)? |
| Data Retention | How long will data be retained? |
| Data Sharing | Who will access the data? Will it be shared externally? |
| Participant Type | Employees, customers, or general public? |
| Location | On-site at participant's workplace? Industrial environment? |
| Existing Agreements | Are there customer NDAs or MSAs that govern research? |

#### 4.1.2 Select Appropriate Consent Form

Based on the assessment above, select the appropriate consent template:

- **Standard Consent Form** (Section 5.1): For most research studies
- **Abbreviated Consent** (Section 5.2): For low-risk surveys or unmoderated studies
- **Enhanced Consent with NDA** (Section 5.3): For research involving proprietary information
- **Site Visit Addendum** (Section 5.4): Additional terms for industrial/workplace visits

#### 4.1.3 Customize the Consent Form

Modify the selected template to accurately reflect:

- Specific study purpose and activities
- Actual recording plans
- True data retention period
- Correct compensation details
- Accurate contact information

**Important:** Never use boilerplate language that does not match your actual study. Consent forms must accurately describe what will happen.

#### 4.1.4 Legal Review (When Required)

Obtain legal review of consent materials when:

- Research involves a new customer segment or geography
- Proprietary information from external companies will be discussed
- Research occurs in regulated industries (healthcare, finance, government)
- The study design is novel or involves elevated risk
- You are uncertain about compliance requirements

Allow 5-10 business days for legal review.

#### 4.1.5 Check Existing Agreements

For B2B research with customers:

1. Check with Sales/Account Management for existing MSAs or NDAs
2. Determine if research is covered under existing agreements
3. Identify any restrictions on recording, data use, or publication
4. Obtain necessary internal approvals before contacting customer participants

### 4.2 Obtaining Consent

#### 4.2.1 Timing of Consent

Consent must be obtained:

- **Before** the research session begins
- **After** the participant has had adequate time to review the consent form
- With sufficient time for the participant to ask questions

Recommended timing:
- **Remote sessions:** Send consent form 24-48 hours in advance; confirm at session start
- **In-person sessions:** Provide form upon arrival; allow 5-10 minutes for review
- **Surveys:** Present consent information on the first screen before any questions

#### 4.2.2 Consent Process for Moderated Sessions

**Step 1: Provide Written Consent Form**

Deliver the consent form via email (remote) or printed copy (in-person). Include:
- The complete consent form
- Contact information for questions
- Clear instructions for signing/returning

**Step 2: Allow Review Time**

Give participants adequate time to read the form completely. Do not rush this process.

**Step 3: Invite Questions**

Before obtaining signature, ask: "Do you have any questions about the consent form or what participation involves?"

Answer all questions honestly and completely. If you cannot answer a question, pause recruitment until you can provide an accurate answer.

**Step 4: Obtain Signature**

Acceptable signature methods:
- Physical signature on printed form
- Electronic signature via DocuSign, Adobe Sign, or similar
- Typed name with checkbox confirmation (for lower-risk studies)
- Recorded verbal consent (see Section 4.2.4)

**Step 5: Provide Copy to Participant**

Ensure the participant retains a copy of the signed consent form for their records.

**Step 6: Verbal Confirmation at Session Start**

At the beginning of the session, verbally confirm:

> "Before we begin, I want to confirm that you received and signed the consent form. As a reminder, this session will be [recorded/not recorded], your participation is voluntary, and you can stop at any time or skip any questions. Do you have any questions before we start?"

If the session is being recorded, start the recording and obtain verbal confirmation on record.

#### 4.2.3 Consent Process for Unmoderated Studies

For unmoderated usability tests, surveys, or diary studies:

**Step 1: Present Consent Information**

Display consent information at the beginning of the study before any data collection. For surveys, this should be the first page.

**Step 2: Require Active Agreement**

Participants must take an active step to indicate consent:
- Checkbox: "I have read and agree to the terms above" (checkbox must not be pre-selected)
- Button: "I agree and wish to continue"

**Step 3: Gate Participation**

Do not allow participants to proceed to the study without completing the consent step. Configure the tool to require consent before continuing.

**Step 4: Provide Reference Information**

Include researcher contact information so participants can ask questions or withdraw consent after participation.

#### 4.2.4 Verbal Consent (When Appropriate)

Verbal consent may be used when:
- Written consent is impractical (e.g., telephone interviews)
- Cultural context makes written consent inappropriate
- The study is low-risk with no sensitive data collection

To obtain verbal consent:

1. Read the key consent elements aloud (purpose, procedures, risks, voluntary nature, recording)
2. Ask if the participant has questions
3. Ask: "Do you agree to participate in this study under these terms?"
4. Record their affirmative response
5. Document in your records that verbal consent was obtained, with date and time

#### 4.2.5 Consent for Observation

When stakeholders, team members, or clients will observe sessions:

1. Include observation in the consent form
2. Inform participants who will be observing (roles, not necessarily names)
3. Give participants the right to decline observation while still participating
4. Introduce observers at the start of in-person sessions

Example language: "Members of our product team may observe this session to better understand user needs. Observers will not participate in the conversation. You may request that observers not be present, and this will not affect your participation or compensation."

### 4.3 During the Research Session

#### 4.3.1 Respecting Participant Rights

Throughout the session, researchers must:

- Remind participants they can skip questions or tasks they're uncomfortable with
- Watch for signs of discomfort and check in as appropriate
- Never pressure participants to complete tasks or answer questions
- Stop recording immediately if requested
- Allow participants to leave at any time without consequence

#### 4.3.2 Handling Unexpected Disclosures

If a participant discloses information outside the scope of consent (e.g., confidential company information you didn't expect):

1. Gently redirect the conversation
2. Note that the disclosure occurred
3. After the session, determine whether the data should be excluded from analysis
4. If in doubt, follow up with the participant about whether they intended to share that information

#### 4.3.3 Protecting Proprietary Information

When research involves proprietary information:

- Remind participants not to share information their employer considers confidential unless authorized
- If proprietary information is shared unexpectedly, note it for review
- Store notes containing proprietary information with appropriate access controls
- Consider whether findings can be shared without revealing confidential details

#### 4.3.4 Documenting Consent Status

Record in your study log:
- Participant identifier
- Date consent was obtained
- Method (written, electronic, verbal)
- Any limitations or modifications to standard consent
- Copy of signed consent form (stored securely)

### 4.4 Post-Session Data Handling

#### 4.4.1 Data Storage Requirements

All research data must be stored according to the following requirements:

| Data Type | Storage Location | Access Controls | Retention Period |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|------------------|
| Signed consent forms | Secure cloud storage (company-approved) | Research team only | 3 years after study completion |
| Session recordings | Secure cloud storage | Research team + approved observers | Delete after analysis, max 12 months |
| Transcripts | Secure cloud storage | Research team | Delete after analysis, max 12 months |
| Analysis notes | Research repository | Research team + stakeholders | As needed for product development |
| Final reports | Research repository | Appropriate internal audiences | Indefinite |

#### 4.4.2 De-identification

Before sharing findings broadly:

1. Remove participant names and replace with identifiers (P1, P2, etc.)
2. Remove or generalize company names for B2B research unless specifically authorized
3. Remove specific job titles if they could identify individuals
4. Review quotes for identifying information
5. Blur faces in screenshots or video clips unless consent explicitly allows identification

#### 4.4.3 Data Disposal

When retention periods expire or participants withdraw consent:

1. Delete recordings from all storage locations
2. Delete transcripts and raw notes
3. Remove identifying information from analysis documents
4. Document that deletion was completed
5. Retain only de-identified, aggregated findings

#### 4.4.4 Handling Withdrawal of Consent

If a participant withdraws consent after the session:

1. Acknowledge the request promptly (within 2 business days)
2. Delete all recordings and transcripts for that participant
3. Remove their data from analysis to the extent practical
4. Confirm deletion in writing to the participant
5. Do not contact them for future research unless they re-consent

### 4.5 Special Situations

#### 4.5.1 Research at Industrial Sites

Additional requirements for research at manufacturing, warehouse, or industrial facilities:

**Pre-Visit:**
- Obtain site-specific safety briefing materials
- Complete required safety training
- Confirm PPE requirements
- Execute any required site-specific waivers or NDAs
- Confirm photography/video permissions (many industrial sites prohibit recording)

**During Visit:**
- Follow all site safety protocols
- Use required PPE at all times
- Obtain consent from all individuals who may be captured in recordings
- Respect restricted areas
- Follow escort requirements

**Documentation:**
- Include site visit terms in consent form (Section 5.4)
- Document safety briefing completion
- Note any site-specific restrictions on data use

#### 4.5.2 Photographing or Recording Work Environments

When you need to photograph or video equipment, processes, or workspaces:

1. Obtain explicit consent for environmental recording separate from participant consent
2. Get approval from site management/facilities
3. Identify any trade secret or confidentiality concerns
4. Agree on which images can be shared externally
5. Provide copies of images to the host organization upon request
6. Delete any images captured inadvertently of restricted areas

#### 4.5.3 Multi-Participant Sessions (Focus Groups)

For focus groups or other multi-participant sessions:

1. All participants must sign consent before the session
2. Include confidentiality expectations in consent: "You agree not to share other participants' comments outside this session"
3. At session start, verbally remind participants that comments are confidential
4. Make clear that you cannot fully guarantee confidentiality since other participants are present
5. Monitor for and address any sharing of sensitive information between participants

#### 4.5.4 Minors or Vulnerable Populations

If research involves minors or vulnerable populations:

1. Do not proceed without explicit legal guidance
2. Obtain parental/guardian consent for minors
3. Obtain assent from the minor participant themselves
4. Apply additional data protections
5. Document additional safeguards in study plan

**Note:** Most B2B and industrial research does not involve vulnerable populations. If you encounter a situation where this might apply, pause and consult with legal.

#### 4.5.5 Research with Employees

When researching with internal employees:

1. Make clear that participation is voluntary and will not affect employment
2. Ensure managers do not pressure direct reports to participate
3. Consider whether employees can be truly anonymous given your organization's size
4. Be cautious about research that might reveal performance or compliance issues
5. Consult HR if the research topic involves workplace issues

**Part B: Confidentiality Provisions**

In addition to the research consent above, both parties agree to the following confidentiality terms:

**1. Confidential Information**

During this research session, each party ("Disclosing Party") may share information that is proprietary or confidential ("Confidential Information"). Confidential Information includes, but is not limited to:

- Business processes, workflows, and procedures
- Product features, roadmaps, or development plans
- Financial information, pricing, or customer data
- Technical specifications, designs, or trade secrets
- Any information identified as confidential at the time of disclosure

**2. Obligations of Receiving Party**

The party receiving Confidential Information ("Receiving Party") agrees to:

- Use Confidential Information only for the purposes of this research study
- Not disclose Confidential Information to third parties without prior written consent
- Protect Confidential Information with at least the same degree of care used to protect its own confidential information
- Limit internal disclosure to individuals who need the information for legitimate research purposes

**3. Exceptions**

These confidentiality obligations do not apply to information that:

- Is or becomes publicly available through no fault of the Receiving Party
- Was known to the Receiving Party prior to disclosure
- Is independently developed without use of Confidential Information
- Is required to be disclosed by law (with prompt notice to the Disclosing Party where permitted)

**4. Term**

These confidentiality obligations shall remain in effect for [2/3/5] years from the date of disclosure.

**5. No License**

Nothing in this agreement grants any license or rights to intellectual property except as necessary to conduct this research.

## 6. Responsibilities

### 6.1 UX Researcher (Study Lead)

- Select appropriate consent template based on study requirements
- Customize consent form to accurately reflect study specifics
- Obtain informed consent from all participants before data collection
- Securely store consent documentation
- Monitor for and respond to participant withdrawals
- Ensure data handling complies with consent terms
- Complete consent tracking log for each study
- Report any consent or ethics concerns to Research Lead

### 6.2 Research Lead / Research Manager

- Review consent forms for high-risk or novel studies
- Maintain and update consent templates
- Determine when legal review is required
- Monitor compliance with consent procedures across studies
- Handle escalated concerns about consent or ethics
- Conduct periodic audits of consent documentation
- Update this SOP as requirements change
- Train new team members on consent procedures

### 6.3 Research Participants (for reference)

Participants have the right to:

- Receive complete information about the study before agreeing
- Ask questions at any time
- Decline to answer specific questions
- Stop participation at any time without penalty
- Request recordings be stopped or deleted
- Receive a copy of their signed consent
- Have their data deleted upon request (within stated limitations)
- Contact the researcher with concerns after the study

### 6.4 Stakeholders/Observers

- Read and understand observer guidelines before observing
- Maintain confidentiality of participant information
- Not contact participants directly without researcher approval
- Respect participant privacy when discussing research findings
- Not share recordings or raw data outside authorized channels

## 7. Quality Checks

### 7.1 Pre-Study Checklist

Before recruiting participants, verify:

- [ ] Appropriate consent template selected for study type
- [ ] All blanks in consent form completed with accurate information
- [ ] Recording and observation sections match actual study plans
- [ ] Data retention period is realistic and compliant with policy
- [ ] Compensation details are accurate and approved
- [ ] Contact information is current
- [ ] Legal review obtained if required (see Section 4.1.4)
- [ ] Existing customer agreements reviewed for B2B studies
- [ ] Site-specific requirements addressed for field research

### 7.2 Per-Participant Checklist

For each participant, confirm:

- [ ] Consent form provided with adequate review time
- [ ] Questions invited and answered before obtaining consent
- [ ] Signature obtained (or verbal consent documented)
- [ ] Copy provided to participant
- [ ] Recording of consent confirmation (for recorded sessions)
- [ ] Entry created in consent tracking log
- [ ] Signed form filed in secure storage

### 7.3 Post-Session Checklist

After each session:

- [ ] Consent form filed in study folder
- [ ] Any consent limitations noted (e.g., "declined observation")
- [ ] Recording stored in approved secure location
- [ ] Access restricted to authorized team members

### 7.4 Study Completion Checklist

When study is complete:

- [ ] All consent forms accounted for and securely stored
- [ ] Data de-identified before broad sharing
- [ ] Recordings deleted per retention policy (or deletion scheduled)
- [ ] Consent tracking log complete and archived
- [ ] Withdrawal requests documented and honored

### 7.5 Periodic Audit (Quarterly)

The Research Lead should verify:

- [ ] Consent forms on file for all studies conducted
- [ ] Electronic storage meets security requirements
- [ ] Retention schedules are being followed
- [ ] Recordings older than retention period have been deleted
- [ ] Team members have completed consent training
- [ ] No outstanding withdrawal requests

> "Of course, let me stop the recording now." [STOP RECORDING] "We can continue with just my notes, or we can end the session - whatever you prefer."

### If Sensitive Information is Shared

> "I appreciate you sharing that. Just to clarify - is that information you're comfortable having included in our research, or would you prefer we consider that off the record?"

### Ending the Session

> "Thank you so much for your time today. As a reminder, your feedback will be combined with other participants and won't be attributed to you by name. If you have any questions later, you can reach me at [email]. [Compensation details if applicable]."

## Appendix B: Common Questions and Answers

**Q: Can I start the session before the consent form is signed if the participant agrees verbally?**
A: No. Written consent must be received before the session begins. For phone interviews where written consent isn't practical, follow the verbal consent procedure (Section 4.2.4) and document it carefully.

**Q: A participant wants to review the consent form "later" and just get started. What do I do?**
A: Politely insist that they review it first. Offer to give them 5 minutes now or reschedule. You cannot proceed without informed consent.

**Q: The participant signed the consent form but now wants to withdraw after the session. What are their rights?**
A: They have the right to withdraw. Delete their recordings and remove their data from analysis to the extent practical. Confirm the deletion in writing.

**Q: Can I share a video clip of one participant in a presentation to another company?**
A: Not without explicit permission. Standard consent typically allows internal use only. You would need additional permission for external sharing.

**Q: A customer shared confidential information about their company. Can I include it in the research report?**
A: Be cautious. Describe findings in generalized terms. If specific details are important, consult with the participant and potentially their organization before including.

**Q: Do I need consent from everyone visible in a recording of a workplace observation?**
A: Yes, anyone identifiable in recordings should consent. For large settings, this may be impractical - consider whether recording is necessary or whether notes would suffice.

**Q: We're doing A/B testing in our live product. Is that "research" requiring consent?**
A: A/B testing of UI changes for optimization is generally covered under product terms of service, not this SOP. However, if you're collecting additional data, surveying users, or the test could cause significant user impact, consult with legal.
