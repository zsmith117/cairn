## 1. Purpose

This Standard Operating Procedure establishes the requirements and procedures for documenting UX research sessions. It ensures that:

- Research data is captured completely and accurately during sessions
- Insights are preserved in a format that enables analysis and synthesis
- Knowledge is transferable across team members and over time
- Collaboration is enabled through shared documentation standards
- Legal and compliance requirements for data preservation are met
- Session data can be effectively analyzed and synthesized
- Participant quotes and behaviors are captured with sufficient context
- Research findings are traceable back to source evidence

**Why This Matters:**

The quality of research documentation directly impacts the quality and credibility of research insights. Poor documentation leads to:
- Lost insights and data that can never be recovered
- Inability to remember what actually happened in sessions
- Difficulty conducting rigorous analysis and synthesis
- Missing participant quotes and critical behavioral observations
- Knowledge gaps when team members transition or leave
- Compliance issues if notes are not properly stored or anonymized
- Wasted research investment due to incomplete or unusable data
- Stakeholder skepticism about research rigor and findings
- Inability to revisit data when new questions emerge
- Missed opportunities to build on past research

Well-documented research serves as a permanent knowledge asset, enabling teams to learn from every session and build understanding over time.

## 3. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Field Notes** | Raw, real-time notes taken during observation or research session; may include shorthand, abbreviations |
| **Observation Notes** | Structured documentation of observed behaviors, actions, and environmental context during sessions |
| **Verbatim Quote** | Exact words spoken by participant, captured word-for-word in quotation marks |
| **Paraphrased Notes** | Researcher's summary of participant's statement in researcher's own words, not in quotes |
| **Timestamping** | Recording the time at which specific events, quotes, or behaviors occurred during a session |
| **Transcription** | Written record of spoken audio, converting recorded speech to text |
| **Session Summary** | Brief synthesis document created immediately after session capturing key insights and takeaways |
| **Research Journal** | Ongoing log where researcher documents patterns, reflections, and methodological decisions across sessions |
| **Reflexivity Notes** | Documentation of researcher's biases, assumptions, and reactions that may influence interpretation |
| **Thick Description** | Rich, detailed documentation that captures context, nuance, and meaning beyond surface observations |
| **Key Moments** | Particularly significant events, quotes, or behaviors during session that warrant special documentation |
| **Post-Session Debrief** | Immediate reflection and discussion after session to capture fresh insights before details fade |
| **Research Artifact** | Physical or digital objects captured during research (photos, screenshots, documents, drawings) |
| **Note Synthesis** | Process of organizing, cleaning, and integrating notes from multiple sources into coherent documentation |
| **Collaborative Notes** | Documentation created by multiple observers simultaneously or sequentially during/after sessions |
| **Session Metadata** | Contextual information about the session (date, time, participant ID, location, moderator, observers) |
| **Contextual Data** | Environmental, situational, and background information that provides meaning to observations |
| **Attribution** | Clear indication of who said or did what, especially important in multi-participant sessions |
| **Note Completeness** | Standard for whether documentation captures sufficient detail to support analysis and knowledge transfer |
| **Anonymization** | Removing or masking personally identifiable information from notes per UXR-002 requirements |

## 4. Procedure

### 4.1 Preparing to Take Notes

#### 4.1.1 Review Research Context

Before each session, prepare by reviewing:

**Required Review:**

| Item | Purpose | What to Check |
|------|---------|---------------|
| Research questions | Focus note-taking on what matters | Primary questions this session should answer |
| Interview/moderation guide | Know what to document | Key topics, questions where quotes are valuable |
| Participant profile | Understand context | Background, role, recruitment criteria, special considerations |
| Prior session notes | Build on learning | Patterns emerging, areas needing more depth |
| Research plan | Understand study goals | Overall objectives, how this session fits |

**Pre-Session Checklist:**

```
BEFORE SESSION - NOTE PREPARATION

[ ] Research questions reviewed and top-of-mind
[ ] Interview guide printed or easily accessible
[ ] Note-taking template ready (see Section 5 Templates)
[ ] Recording equipment tested (if recording)
[ ] Participant ID and basic info noted in header
[ ] Any special flagging needs identified (e.g., specific topics to capture)
[ ] Backup note-taking method ready (if technical issues occur)
[ ] Clear on who's taking notes vs. moderating (if team session)
```

#### 4.1.2 Set Up Note-Taking Tools

**Tool Setup:**

| Format | Tools | Setup Required |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| **Digital notes** | Laptop, tablet, note-taking app | - Create session-specific document<br>- Insert template<br>- Test typing speed and comfort<br>- Silence notifications<br>- Position screen so visible to notetaker, not distracting to participant |
| **Handwritten notes** | Notebook, pens, guide with margins | - Prepare clean page or template<br>- Multiple pens ready<br>- Guide printed with wide margins for annotations<br>- Good writing surface |
| **Hybrid** | Printed guide + laptop | - Printed guide for annotations during session<br>- Laptop for detailed notes and post-session documentation |

**Positioning Considerations:**

- In-person: Sit at slight angle so note-taking doesn't create barrier; participant should feel you're engaged with them
- Remote: Second monitor ideal for notes; if single screen, place note window where you can see participant video and notes
- Test setup before participant joins to ensure smooth workflow

#### 4.1.3 Establish Note-Taking Roles (If Multiple Observers)

When multiple team members observe:

**Role Clarity:**

| Role | Responsibilities | Note-Taking Focus |
|------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Moderator** | Lead session, ask questions, maintain rapport | Light notes on guide; key quotes and moments only |
| **Primary Notetaker** | Comprehensive documentation | Detailed notes on responses, behaviors, context |
| **Secondary Observer** | Focused documentation | Specific aspects (e.g., usability issues, quotes, body language) |
| **Stakeholder Observer** | Silent observation | Own notes for their use; not official documentation |

**Pre-Session Alignment:**

Before session, team confirms:
- Who is taking primary notes
- What each person will focus on documenting
- How notes will be consolidated after session
- Debrief timing and location

### 4.2 Note-Taking During Sessions

#### 4.2.1 Note-Taking During Interviews

**What to Capture:**

| Category | What to Document | How to Document |
|----------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Verbatim quotes** | Memorable, vivid, representative statements | Use quotation marks; capture exact words; note timestamp or question |
| **Paraphrased responses** | Main points of participant responses | Summarize key ideas; no quotation marks; shorter than verbatim |
| **Behaviors** | Tone, emotion, body language, hesitations, reactions | Brief descriptive notes: "laughed," "long pause," "frustrated tone" |
| **Context** | Environmental factors, interruptions, relevant background | Note what's happening around conversation that affects it |
| **Your observations** | Patterns, surprises, contradictions | Bracket or mark as observer comment: [Note: contradicts earlier statement] |

**Real-Time Note Structure:**

```
SESSION: Interview with P07 | 2026-01-26 | Moderator: [Name] | Notetaker: [Name]

SECTION: [Topic Area from Guide]

Q: [Question asked - can be abbreviated]
- Main response point 1
- "Exact quote if notable"
- Response point 2, including context or behavior [excited tone]

[Note: Pattern emerging - 3rd participant to mention X]

Q: [Next question]
- Response
  - Probed on Y: more detail
- "Another relevant quote"

KEY MOMENT (15:30): Participant struggled with [task]; tried 3 approaches before giving up
```

**Balancing Attention:**

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|----------|
| Missing quotes while writing | Capture key phrases, not every word; can fill in from recording later if recording |
| Can't keep up with pace | Use abbreviations (see Appendix A); focus on novel information; let some paraphrasing be very brief |
| Maintaining eye contact | Look up regularly; participants understand you're taking notes; brief glances down are fine |
| Moderating while noting | If you're moderator, keep notes light; focus on key quotes and moments; rely on observer or recording |

**Abbreviations and Shorthand:**

Develop consistent abbreviations to increase speed:
- P = Participant
- R = Researcher
- b/c = because
- w/ = with
- re: = regarding
- → = leads to, results in
- !! = important/surprising
- ? = unclear, need to follow up
- (See Appendix A for complete abbreviation list)

#### 4.2.2 Note-Taking During Usability Tests

Usability test notes focus on observation of behavior and task performance.

**Observation Notes Structure:**

```
TASK [N]: [Task Name] | Start: [Time]

SCENARIO PROVIDED: [Brief scenario given to participant]

OBSERVATIONS:
- Started by [action]
- Looked for [element] in [location] - didn't find it
- Clicked [element] - expected [X], saw [Y]
- "Hmm, where would that be?" [verbalized thought]
- Tried [approach 1] - didn't work
- Paused 15 seconds, looking around screen
- Eventually clicked [element] - success
- Completion time: 3:45

TASK OUTCOME: [ ] Success [ ] Success with difficulty [X] Partial [ ] Failure

POST-TASK RATING: Difficulty 4/5 (1=very easy, 5=very difficult)
POST-TASK COMMENT: "I expected it to be in [location], not [actual location]"

ISSUES IDENTIFIED:
[1] Severity: High | Element [X] not discoverable in expected location
[2] Severity: Medium | Label [Y] ambiguous, unclear what it does
```

**What to Observe and Document:**

| Category | What to Capture | Example |
|----------|------------------|---------|
| **Starting behavior** | Where participant looks/clicks first | "Started by clicking primary nav item" |
| **Path taken** | Sequence of actions | "Home → Search → Filter → Product page → Add to cart" |
| **Hesitations** | Pauses, uncertainty, scanning | "Paused 10 sec scanning page before clicking" |
| **Errors** | Wrong clicks, dead ends, confusion | "Clicked 'Settings' thinking it would open profile" |
| **Verbalizations** | What participant says while working | "I would expect this to be under..." |
| **Emotions** | Frustration, satisfaction, surprise | "Laughed when found it: 'Of course it's there'" |
| **Recovery** | How they correct errors | "Used browser back button to return" |
| **Success** | How they accomplished goal | "Found via search instead of navigation" |

**Issue Documentation:**

Track usability issues in structured format:

| Issue # | Task | Severity | Description | Timestamp |
|---------|------|----------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Task 2 | Critical | Cannot complete checkout - button inactive | 12:34 |
| 2 | Task 1 | Serious | Search results confusing - mixed types not labeled | 08:15 |
| 3 | Task 3 | Minor | Label text small, hard to read | 18:22 |

**Severity Definitions:**
- **Critical**: Prevents task completion; no workaround available
- **Serious**: Causes significant difficulty or errors; workaround exists but not obvious
- **Minor**: Causes confusion or inconvenience; doesn't prevent completion

#### 4.2.3 Note-Taking During Focus Groups

Focus groups require tracking multiple participants and group dynamics.

**Multi-Participant Tracking:**

```
FOCUS GROUP: [Topic] | Date | Moderator: [Name] | Participants: 6

PARTICIPANT KEY:
P1 - Female, 30s, Heavy user
P2 - Male, 40s, New user
P3 - Female, 20s, Moderate user
[etc.]

---

TOPIC: [Discussion topic]

P1: "I use it every day for [purpose]" [enthusiastic]
P3: "Same, but I find [feature] frustrating" [others nodding]
P2: [contrasting view] "I haven't had that problem, I use it differently"
P5: [builds on P3] "Yes! That's exactly my issue too"

GROUP DYNAMIC NOTE: Strong consensus emerging on frustration with [feature];
P2 appears to be outlier in experience

---

P4: [introducing new idea] "What about [suggestion]?"
[Group discusses; 3 people speak at once]
- General reaction: Interest mixed with concern about [aspect]
P1: "That would solve [problem] but might create [new problem]"
```

**Focus Group Documentation Strategies:**

| Challenge | Strategy |
|-----------|----------|
| **Multiple speakers at once** | Note "Multiple voices agreeing" or "Crosstalk - general agreement on X" |
| **Tracking who said what** | Use participant codes (P1, P2); essential for attribution |
| **Group dynamics** | Document reactions of others: "others nodding," "disagreement," "laughter" |
| **Dominant voices** | Note participation patterns: "P1 speaking frequently; P5 has been quiet" |
| **Side conversations** | Try to capture briefly: "P2 and P4 discussing X during main conversation" |
| **Non-verbal** | Note significant body language: "collective laughter," "head shakes from 3 people" |

**Capturing Agreement/Disagreement:**

- Document when consensus emerges
- Note dissenting views even if minority
- Capture language participants use to build on each other's ideas
- Flag when one person's comment shifts group thinking

#### 4.2.4 Note-Taking During Contextual Inquiry

Field research emphasizes environmental context and workflow observation.

**Field Notes Structure:**

```
CONTEXTUAL INQUIRY: [Activity] | Participant: [ID] | Location: [Place] | Date

ENVIRONMENTAL CONTEXT:
- Setting: [Description of physical space]
- Tools visible: [List physical tools, devices, materials in workspace]
- Layout: [Sketch or description of workspace organization]
- Ambient conditions: [Noise level, lighting, temperature if relevant]
- Other people present: [Who else is around, their roles]

---

OBSERVATION LOG:

[09:15] Arrives at workstation; checks [system] first thing
- Has dual monitors; keeps [X] on left, [Y] on right
- "I always start by checking [X] to see what came in overnight"

[09:18] Receives phone call; interruption
- Switches to [different task] while on call
- Keeps [system] visible while talking
- [Note: Frequent interruptions seem normal here]

[09:25] Returns to original task
- Opens [tool], starts [process]
- Workflow: [step 1] → [step 2] → [step 3]
- Uses printed reference guide taped to monitor edge [PHOTO 001]
- "I made this cheat sheet because I could never remember the codes"

[09:32] Encounters error
- Tries [solution 1] - doesn't work
- Checks informal notes in notebook [PHOTO 002]
- Asks colleague at next desk for help
- [Note: Reliance on colleague knowledge vs. official documentation]

[SKETCH 001: Workflow diagram showing information flow]
```

**What to Document in Field Research:**

| Category | What to Capture | Documentation Method |
|----------|------------------|----------------------|
| **Physical environment** | Workspace layout, tools, artifacts | Description + photos + sketches |
| **Workflow sequence** | Order of activities, steps taken | Timestamped observations + diagram |
| **Information sources** | Where information comes from | List + note frequency of use |
| **Workarounds** | Adaptations, unofficial methods | Detailed description + rationale |
| **Interruptions** | How work is disrupted and resumed | Note frequency and handling |
| **Collaboration** | Interactions with others | Who, when, for what purpose |
| **Artifacts** | Forms, tools, personal organization systems | Photos + description of use |
| **Quotes** | Explanations of why they do things | Verbatim capture of reasoning |

**Photo and Artifact Logging:**

```
ARTIFACT LOG:

Photo 001: Reference guide taped to monitor
- Type: Personal cheat sheet
- Content: System codes and shortcuts
- Context: Created by participant because official documentation too complex
- Relevance: Shows gap in official support materials

Photo 002: Personal notebook
- Type: Informal reference
- Content: Notes on how to solve common problems
- Context: Accumulated over 2 years; shared with new team members
- Relevance: Informal knowledge transfer system

Artifact 003: Printed form (copied, not photographed due to PII)
- Type: Manual workflow tracking form
- Content: Checklist for process steps
- Context: Created by team because system doesn't track this
- Relevance: Manual overhead required to compensate for system gaps
```

**Real-Time Questioning During Observation:**

Ask questions while observing:
- "What are you doing now?"
- "Why did you do it that way?"
- "What are you looking for?"
- "What happens next?"
- "Is this typical or unusual today?"
- "Where did you learn to do it that way?"
- "What would you do if [scenario]?"

### 4.3 Note-Taking Methods and Approaches

#### 4.3.1 Structured vs. Unstructured Notes

**Structured Notes:**

Use templates with predefined sections:

| Advantages | Disadvantages | When to Use |
|------------|---------------|-------------|
| Consistent across sessions | Can feel constraining | Multi-session studies needing comparison |
| Ensures completeness | May miss unexpected insights outside structure | Multiple notetakers needing consistency |
| Easy to scan later | Takes more setup | Usability tests with task structure |
| Supports analysis | Less flexible during session | Structured interviews |

**Unstructured Notes:**

Free-form documentation:

| Advantages | Disadvantages | When to Use |
|------------|---------------|-------------|
| Flexible, adaptive | Inconsistent across sessions | Exploratory, generative research |
| Captures unexpected | May miss important areas | Single researcher studies |
| Fast during session | Harder to analyze later | Field research with unpredictable flow |
| Natural flow | Knowledge transfer harder | Experienced researchers |

**Recommendation:**

- Use structured templates for most research (see Section 5)
- Allow flexibility within structure
- Balance consistency with adaptability

#### 4.3.2 Cornell Method Adaptation for Research

Adapt the Cornell note-taking method for research sessions:

```
SESSION NOTES                                  | CUES & TAGS
-----------------------------------------------|-------------------
Main notes go here                             | - Keywords
Quotes, observations, responses                | - Themes
Behaviors, context                             | - Questions
                                               | - Patterns
                                               | - !!Important
                                               |
                                               |
-----------------------------------------------|-------------------
SUMMARY (added post-session):
Key takeaways, main insights, patterns observed
```

**How to Use:**

- **During session**: Take notes in main area on left
- **During or immediately after**: Add tags, keywords, flags on right
- **After session**: Write summary at bottom

**Benefits:**

- Built-in space for meta-notes and tagging
- Easy to scan for topics using right column
- Forces post-session reflection via summary

#### 4.3.3 Verbatim vs. Paraphrased Notes

**When to Capture Verbatim:**

| Situation | Why Verbatim Matters | Example |
|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| Vivid, memorable language | Preserves participant voice | "It's like trying to nail jello to the wall" |
| Key insights | Exact words are evidence | "I would never use that feature - it's solving the wrong problem" |
| Surprising statements | Capture unexpected finding | "I actually prefer the old version" |
| Contradictions | Document exact claim | "I do that every day" [contradicts earlier statement] |
| Representative quotes | Will use in reports | "We waste 3 hours a week on this workaround" |

**When Paraphrasing Is Fine:**

| Situation | Why Paraphrase | Example |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| Routine information | Not unique insight | Role description, background details |
| Rambling responses | Participant wasn't concise | Long tangent → summarize main point |
| Already well-documented | Confirms known information | Participant describes documented workflow |
| Speed is critical | Can't keep up with typing | Fast-paced usability test observations |

**How to Distinguish in Notes:**

```
- "Exact words in quotes" = verbatim
- Paraphrased summary without quotes = paraphrase
- [Brackets for notetaker observations] = meta-notes
```

#### 4.3.4 Timestamping Techniques

Timestamps enable locating moments in recordings and tracking timing:

**When to Timestamp:**

- Beginning of each major topic/task
- Key quotes you want to find again
- Critical moments (frustration, success, surprise)
- Usability issues
- Changes in emotional tone
- Any moment you might want to create a video clip of later

**Timestamping Methods:**

| Method | How It Works | When to Use |
|--------|--------------|-------------|
| **Manual timestamps** | Note time from clock/recording timer at key moments | Most common; works for any session |
| **Periodic timestamps** | Note time every 5-10 minutes as section headers | Helps locate general area in recording |
| **Event-based timestamps** | Only for significant moments | When you only want to mark key events |
| **Audio note marks** | Record "Timestamp" into audio for later finding | Remote sessions; hands-free marking |

**Example with Timestamps:**

```
[08:30] TOPIC: Current workflow

Q: Walk me through your typical process
[09:15] !! "The system crashes at least once a day" - frustrated tone
Response: [describes workaround]
[11:42] TASK 1: Create new project
- Started by clicking [X]
[14:20] ERROR: Button didn't respond, confusion
```

#### 4.3.5 Tagging and Coding While Noting

Light tagging during sessions helps later analysis:

**Simple Tagging System:**

| Tag | Meaning | When to Use |
|-----|---------|-------------|
| !! | Important / surprising | Novel insight, unexpected finding |
| ? | Question / unclear / follow-up needed | Something to clarify later |
| PAIN | Pain point | Problem or frustration mentioned |
| WORKAROUND | Workaround or hack | Participant has adapted to problem |
| WISH | Desired feature or improvement | "I wish it could..." |
| QUOTE | Good quote for reports | Vivid, representative language |
| PATTERN | Similar to other sessions | Recurring theme |
| [THEME] | Tag with theme name | [MOBILE], [WORKFLOW], [COLLABORATION] |

**Example with Tags:**

```
P: "I have to manually export to Excel, clean the data, then import to the dashboard"
[WORKAROUND] [PAIN] [WORKFLOW]
!! "We waste probably 5 hours a week on this"

P: "I wish it could just sync automatically"
[WISH] [INTEGRATION]
```

**Benefits:**

- Makes analysis faster
- Highlights key moments
- Creates scannable notes
- Enables quick pattern recognition across sessions

**Caution:**

- Don't let tagging slow down note-taking
- Tags can be added post-session if needed
- Keep tag system simple (5-10 tags maximum during session)

#### 4.3.6 Digital vs. Handwritten Notes

**Digital Notes:**

| Advantages | Disadvantages | Best For |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| Fast typing | Screen creates barrier (in-person) | Remote sessions |
| Easy to search later | Tech failures possible | Fast-paced sessions |
| Can copy/paste quotes from transcript | Can feel less personal | Multiple long sessions |
| Easy to share | Requires charged device | Usability tests (capture issues efficiently) |
| Timestamping easier | Temptation to multitask | |

**Handwritten Notes:**

| Advantages | Disadvantages | Best For |
|------------|---------------|----------|
| More personal, warm | Slower than typing | In-person rapport-building sessions |
| No tech failures | Harder to search later | Field research (more portable) |
| Easy to sketch/diagram | Must transcribe to share | Short sessions |
| Less barrier with participant | Can't read own handwriting sometimes | Sensitive topics (less formal feel) |

**Hybrid Approach (Recommended):**

- **During session**: Use printed interview guide with wide margins for handwritten annotations
- **Immediately after**: Transfer key points and quotes to digital document while fresh
- **Best of both**: Personal during session, searchable after

### 4.4 Post-Session Documentation

#### 4.4.1 Immediate Post-Session Tasks (Within 1 Hour)

Complete these tasks immediately while memory is fresh:

**Post-Session Checklist (Within 1 Hour):**

```
IMMEDIATELY AFTER SESSION:

[ ] Save recording and backup immediately (if recorded)
[ ] Transfer recording to secure storage per UXR-002
[ ] Complete session metadata (duration, participant ID, any issues)
[ ] Clean up notes for readability
  [ ] Expand abbreviations that won't be clear later
  [ ] Add bracketed clarifications where needed
  [ ] Mark incomplete sections that need filling from recording
[ ] Capture key quotes you remember but didn't write down fully
[ ] Note any behavioral observations you couldn't type during session
[ ] Complete session summary (Template 5.4)
[ ] Document key insights while fresh
[ ] Note any methodological issues or guide adjustments needed
[ ] Flag any follow-up questions to explore in future sessions
[ ] Share session summary with team (if collaborative study)
```

**Why This Matters:**

Research shows that memory fades rapidly:
- 50% of details forgotten within 1 hour
- 70% forgotten within 24 hours
- 90% forgotten within 1 week

Immediate documentation is critical to preserve insights.


**Distribution:**

- Share with research team immediately
- Add to session folder with detailed notes
- Use to inform subsequent sessions

#### 4.4.3 Post-Session Reflection

Beyond the summary, document your reflections:

**Reflection Prompts:**

| Category | Reflection Questions |
|----------|---------------------|
| **Patterns** | - What patterns am I seeing across sessions?<br>- How does this connect to previous sessions?<br>- What themes are emerging? |
| **Surprises** | - What surprised me?<br>- What contradicted my expectations?<br>- What did I learn that I didn't expect? |
| **Hypotheses** | - What hypotheses am I forming?<br>- What do I want to test in next sessions?<br>- What questions remain? |
| **Bias check** | - What did I expect to hear vs. what I actually heard?<br>- Am I seeing what I want to see?<br>- What contradicts my assumptions? |
| **Methodology** | - What worked well in this session?<br>- What would I change?<br>- How can I improve note-taking? |

**Research Journal:**

Maintain an ongoing research journal across the study:

```
RESEARCH JOURNAL: [Study Name]

[Date] - Session [N]

PATTERNS EMERGING:
- Seeing consistency around [theme] (now 4/6 participants)
- Variation appearing based on [variable]

WORKING HYPOTHESES:
- It seems that [hypothesis] - need to test more explicitly in remaining sessions

REFLEXIVITY:
- I noticed I was surprised when participant said [X] - realized I had assumed [Y]
- Need to check my bias around [topic]

METHODOLOGICAL NOTES:
- Question 3 consistently not yielding useful info - rephrase for remaining sessions
- Timing working well with new guide

THINGS TO EXPLORE:
- Multiple participants mentioning [X] in passing - should probe more directly
- Curious about connection between [Y] and [Z]
```

#### 4.4.4 Note Cleanup and Organization

Transform raw session notes into polished documentation:

**Cleanup Process:**

| Raw Notes | Cleaned Notes |
|-----------|---------------|
| Abbreviations and shorthand | Full words written out |
| Fragmented sentences | Complete sentences |
| Unclear references ("that thing") | Specific references ("[feature name]") |
| Missing context | Added bracketed context |
| Messy structure | Organized by topic/question |
| Your mid-session thoughts mixed in | Clearly marked as [Observer note] |

**Example Transformation:**

**Before (raw notes):**
```
P says use it 3x/day, bc mgr requires report
- frustrated re: time
- "pain to export"
- tried asking IT for auto but no
wishes it was easier
```

**After (cleaned):**
```
Q: How often do you use [System X]?

Participant uses system 3 times per day because manager requires daily report.
Expresses frustration about time required.

"It's a pain to export the data every time" - frustrated tone

Participant has requested IT create an automated export but request was declined.

WISH: "I wish there was an easier way to get the data out"
```

**Balance:**

- Clean for readability and knowledge transfer
- Don't over-edit; preserve authenticity
- Add clarifications in [brackets] so original content is distinguishable
- Don't delete anything; you might need it later

### 4.5 Transcription Management

#### 4.5.1 When to Transcribe

Transcription is resource-intensive. Decide based on:

**Transcribe When:**

| Situation | Why Transcribe | Type of Transcription |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Generative research | Need verbatim quotes for analysis | Full transcript |
| Academic/formal research | Rigor requires complete record | Full, verbatim transcript |
| Sensitive/high-stakes | Accuracy is critical | Full, verbatim transcript |
| Complex analysis planned | Will be coding and analyzing deeply | Full transcript |
| Knowledge transfer critical | Other team members need full context | Full transcript |

**Don't Necessarily Transcribe When:**

| Situation | Alternative | Why Alternative Works |
|-----------|-------------|----------------------|
| Usability testing with screen recording | Video with timestamped notes | Visual record more valuable than words |
| Clear notes with key quotes already captured | Detailed notes sufficient | Transcription doesn't add enough value |
| Quick tactical research | Summary document adequate | Speed matters more than completeness |
| Budget/time constrained | Selective transcription of key segments | Focus resources on highest-value portions |

**Selective Transcription:**

Instead of full transcription, transcribe only:
- Key quotes flagged during session
- Segments where detailed language matters
- Sections that were unclear in notes
- Most valuable 20% of session

#### 4.5.2 Manual vs. Automated Transcription

**Automated Transcription:**

| Service | Pros | Cons | When to Use |
|---------|------|------|-------------|
| Otter.ai, Rev Auto, Descript | Fast (minutes) | Lower accuracy | Clear audio; first pass before manual review |
| | Affordable | Struggles with accents, jargon | High volume of sessions |
| | Integrated with recording | Requires editing | Budget constrained |

**Manual Transcription:**

| Service | Pros | Cons | When to Use |
|---------|------|------|-------------|
| Rev Human, Professional transcription | High accuracy | Expensive | Critical research |
| | Handles accents, jargon | Slower (days) | Budget available |
| Self-transcription | Complete control | Very time-consuming | Learning deeply from data |
| | Deepest immersion in data | Not scalable | Small studies |

**Hybrid Approach (Recommended):**

1. Use automated transcription for first pass
2. Review and correct transcript manually
3. Focus manual effort on most important sections
4. Accept minor errors in less critical sections

**Cost-Benefit Analysis:**

- Full professional transcription: ~$1-2 per audio minute
- Automated transcription: ~$0.10-0.25 per minute
- Time to review/correct automated: ~15-30 minutes per hour of audio
- Time to fully self-transcribe: ~4-6 hours per hour of audio

#### 4.5.3 Transcription Quality Standards

**Transcript Quality Levels:**

| Level | Description | Accuracy | Use Case |
|-------|-------------|----------|----------|
| **Verbatim** | Every word, including filler words, false starts, repetitions | Highest | Formal research, linguistic analysis |
| **Clean verbatim** | Exact words but remove "um," "uh," false starts | High | Most qualitative research |
| **Intelligent verbatim** | Exact meaning but edited for readability | Medium-High | Reports, stakeholder sharing |
| **Summary** | Key points captured, not word-for-word | Medium | Quick reference, secondary sessions |

**Recommended Standard:**

Use **clean verbatim** for most research:
- Captures exact words
- Removes distracting filler that doesn't add meaning
- Readable and analyzable
- Preserves participant voice

**Quality Check for Transcripts:**

```
TRANSCRIPT REVIEW CHECKLIST:

[ ] Speaker attribution correct throughout
[ ] Timestamps included (at least every minute or at topic changes)
[ ] Inaudible sections marked clearly: [inaudible] or [00:15:30 - unclear]
[ ] Non-verbal cues noted: [laughs], [long pause], [frustrated tone]
[ ] Technical terms spelled correctly
[ ] Participant ID used consistently (not real name)
[ ] Anonymization complete per UXR-002 (names, companies, PII removed)
[ ] Formatting consistent and readable
[ ] Major errors corrected
```

#### 4.5.4 Reviewing and Correcting Transcripts

Even automated transcripts require review:

**Review Process:**

1. **Listen while reading** (at 1.5-2x speed)
2. **Correct critical errors:**
   - Misheard technical terms
   - Wrong names of products/tools/companies
   - Errors that change meaning
3. **Let minor errors go:**
   - Small grammatical mistakes that don't affect meaning
   - Filler word variations (um/uh)
4. **Add valuable context:**
   - [laughs], [sarcastic tone], [long pause]
   - [participant struggles with interface]
5. **Verify anonymization:**
   - Real names replaced with P1, P2, etc.
   - Company names replaced with [Company] or [Competitor]
   - Personally identifiable information removed

**Time Allocation:**

- Budget 15-30 minutes review time per hour of transcribed audio
- Focus most attention on sections you'll analyze or quote from
- Lighter review of secondary sections

#### 4.5.5 Anonymization in Transcripts

Per UXR-002, remove or mask PII:

**What to Anonymize:**

| Type | Original | Anonymized |
|------|----------|------------|
| Participant name | "I'm Sarah Johnson" | "I'm [Participant name]" or remove entirely |
| Company names | "We use Salesforce" | "We use [CRM system]" |
| Colleague names | "My manager Bob said..." | "My manager [Name] said..." |
| Specific locations | "Our Chicago office" | "Our [City] office" or "One of our offices" |
| Dates that identify | "I started March 15th" | "I started [Date]" or "I started recently" |
| Email addresses | "contact me at sarah@..." | [removed] |
| Phone numbers | Mentioned in audio | [removed] |

**How to Anonymize:**

- Use consistent codes: P1, P2, P3 for participants
- Use brackets for removed content: [Company], [Manager name]
- Use generic terms: [CRM], [analytics tool], [City]
- Document anonymization key separately (stored securely per UXR-002)

**Anonymization Key (stored separately, securely):**

```
PARTICIPANT KEY - CONFIDENTIAL

P1 = Sarah Johnson, Company A
P2 = Michael Chen, Company B
P3 = Jennifer Garcia, Company C

[Tool X] = Salesforce
[Tool Y] = Tableau
[City A] = Chicago
```

### 4.7 Collaborative Note-Taking

#### 4.7.1 Multiple Observers in Sessions

When multiple team members observe:

**Division of Responsibilities:**

| Role | Primary Focus | Note-Taking Approach |
|------|---------------|----------------------|
| **Moderator** | Leading session, rapport | Light notes: key quotes, surprises, moments to revisit |
| **Primary notetaker** | Comprehensive documentation | Detailed notes on all responses, behaviors, context |
| **Secondary observer** | Specific focus area | Targeted notes: usability issues OR specific theme OR body language |
| **Stakeholder observer** | Learning, specific questions | Personal notes for their reference; not official record |

**Pre-Session Coordination:**

Before session, team aligns on:

```
TEAM NOTE-TAKING ROLES - Session [N]

Moderator: [Name] - Will focus on moderating; light notes only
Primary Notetaker: [Name] - Comprehensive notes using Template 5.1
Secondary Observer: [Name] - Focus on [specific area: usability issues]
Stakeholder: [Name] - Observing; personal notes

AFTER SESSION:
- Debrief location: [Meeting room]
- Debrief time: Immediately after (15 min)
- Notetaker will share draft notes within 2 hours
- Team will review and add observations by end of day
```

#### 4.7.2 Note Sharing Protocols

**Sharing Timeline:**

| Milestone | Timing | What to Share | With Whom |
|-----------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| During session | Real-time (if remote) | Collaborative doc (Google Doc) | Research team only |
| Immediately after | Within 1-2 hours | Cleaned session notes | Research team |
| Same day | Within 6 hours | Session summary | Research team + stakeholders |
| Within 24 hours | Next day | Final session notes | Research team |
| Weekly | End of week | Week's session summaries | Stakeholders |

**Sharing Method:**

- **Internal research team**: Shared folder with full access
- **Stakeholders**: Email summary or shared read-only doc
- **Broader team**: Final outputs only, anonymized

#### 4.7.3 Synchronous vs. Asynchronous Collaboration

**Synchronous (During Session):**

**Remote Research - Collaborative Note-Taking:**

| Approach | How It Works | Pros | Cons |
|----------|--------------|------|------|
| Shared Google Doc | All observers type in same doc simultaneously | All observations captured; diverse perspectives | Can be chaotic; need discipline |
| Dovetail/UserTesting | Platform with built-in note collaboration | Structured; integrated with video | Platform-specific; cost |
| Slack channel | Observers post observations in channel | Fast; informal | Unstructured; scattered |

**Best Practices for Real-Time Collaboration:**

- Assign sections of doc to different people
- Use initials to identify who wrote what: [JD] observation
- One person focuses on verbatim quotes; another on behavior
- Agree on tagging system before session

**Asynchronous (After Session):**

| Approach | How It Works | When to Use |
|----------|--------------|-------------|
| Lead notetaker drafts, others review | One person creates comprehensive notes; team adds comments/observations | Most common; clear ownership |
| Round-robin | Each observer documents different sections | Multiple observers at in-person session |
| Additive | Primary notes + supplemental observation docs from other observers | Each person has specific focus area |

**Review and Consolidation:**

After session:

1. **Primary notetaker** shares draft within 2 hours
2. **Observers** add comments/observations within 24 hours
3. **Lead researcher** incorporates feedback and finalizes notes
4. **Team** reviews final notes for accuracy

#### 4.7.4 Debrief Meetings

**Immediate Post-Session Debrief:**

Hold brief (10-15 min) debrief immediately after session:

**Debrief Structure:**

```
POST-SESSION DEBRIEF - Session [N]

1. INITIAL REACTIONS (3 min)
   - What stood out to each person?
   - First impressions?

2. KEY INSIGHTS (5 min)
   - What did we learn?
   - What surprised us?
   - What confirmed or contradicted expectations?

3. OBSERVATIONS WE WANT TO ENSURE WE CAPTURE (3 min)
   - Anything someone noticed that might not be in notes?
   - Body language, tone, context?

4. IMPLICATIONS FOR NEXT SESSIONS (2 min)
   - Should we adjust guide?
   - Anything to probe more deeply in future?

5. ACTION ITEMS (2 min)
   - Who's finalizing notes? By when?
   - Any immediate follow-up needed?
```

**Benefits of Immediate Debrief:**

- Captures observations not in notes
- Surfaces different perspectives
- Ensures alignment on key takeaways
- Identifies guide adjustments while fresh

**Weekly Synthesis Meeting:**

If study has many sessions, hold weekly synthesis:

- Review patterns emerging across sessions
- Discuss working hypotheses
- Align on what to explore more deeply
- Share research journal observations

#### 4.7.6 Knowledge Transfer

Make notes accessible and understandable to others:

**Knowledge Transfer Principles:**

| Principle | How to Apply |
|-----------|--------------|

| **Explain abbreviations** | First use or in header: "P = Participant, CRM = Customer Relationship Management system" |

| **Summarize key points** | Session summary makes notes accessible without reading everything |

5. Debrief together after their first observation

### 4.8 Quality Standards for Notes

#### 4.8.1 Completeness Criteria

**Quality Note Completeness:**

| Criterion | Standard | How to Check |
|-----------|----------|--------------|
| **Coverage** | All major questions/topics addressed | Compare notes to interview guide; confirm all sections documented |
| **Responses** | Main points of participant responses captured | Can you reconstruct what participant said about each topic? |
| **Quotes** | Key quotes captured verbatim | At least 5-10 notable quotes per 60-min session |
| **Behavior** | Notable behaviors, tone, reactions documented | Notes include more than just words; capture how things were said |
| **Context** | Sufficient context to interpret responses | Someone not at session can understand meaning |
| **Surprises** | Unexpected or novel information flagged | Clear what was surprising or different from expectations |
| **Metadata** | Session info complete | Participant ID, date, moderator, duration, any issues |

**Minimum Documentation Standard:**

Even for brief or informal sessions, capture at minimum:
- Session metadata (who, when, how long)
- 3-5 key insights
- Notable quotes (at least 3-5)
- Any surprises or novel information
- Next steps or follow-up questions

#### 4.8.2 Clarity and Readability

**Readability Standards:**

| Aspect | Standard | Example |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Sentence structure** | Complete sentences for important content | ✓ "Participant uses system 3 times daily"<br>✗ "uses 3x/day" |
| **Abbreviations** | Expanded on first use or commonly understood | ✓ "CRM (Customer Relationship Management)"<br>✗ "CRMNG sys" |
| **References** | Specific, not vague | ✓ "feature X in navigation menu"<br>✗ "that thing" |
| **Context** | Added in brackets when needed | ✓ "It's frustrating [referring to export process]"<br>✗ "It's frustrating" |
| **Attribution** | Clear who said/did what | ✓ "P: 'I use it daily' M: 'How do you use it?'"<br>✗ "I use it daily" |

**Readability Test:**

Could someone who wasn't at the session understand the notes and derive insights?

#### 4.8.3 Accuracy Verification

**Accuracy Check Methods:**

| Method | When to Use | How to Do It |
|--------|-------------|--------------|
| **Listen-back** | Important quotes or unclear moments | Compare notes to recording; correct as needed |
| **Peer review** | Critical research; training new notetakers | Have colleague check notes against recording |
| **Participant validation** | High-stakes research; academic | Share summary with participant for verification (rarely done) |
| **Cross-reference** | Multiple notetakers | Compare notes from different observers; reconcile discrepancies |

**Common Accuracy Issues:**

| Issue | How to Prevent | How to Correct |
|-------|----------------|----------------|
| Misheard words | Use recording for important quotes | Re-listen and correct |
| Misinterpreted meaning | Probe for clarification during session | Add [Note: possibly means...] if uncertain |
| Missed information | Have multiple observers if possible | Mark gaps: [missed response - see recording at 15:30] |
| Conflated speakers | Note speaker for each statement | Re-listen if attributions unclear |

#### 4.8.4 Quote Attribution

**Attribution Standards:**

```
CLEAR ATTRIBUTION:

✓ P: "I use the system every day" [P = Participant]
✓ P2: "I disagree, I only use it weekly" [P2 = Participant 2 in focus group]
✓ R: "Can you explain more about that?" [R = Researcher]

UNCLEAR ATTRIBUTION:

✗ "I use the system every day" [Who said this?]
✗ Someone mentioned they use it daily [Who?]
✗ They said it's frustrating [Who are "they"?]
```

**Attribution in Multi-Participant Sessions:**

- Use consistent codes: P1, P2, P3 or descriptive: P-Manager, P-Operator
- Create participant key at top of notes
- When multiple people speak at once: "Multiple participants agreed..."

#### 4.8.5 Context Preservation

**Context to Capture:**

| Context Type | What to Document | Why It Matters |
|--------------|------------------|----------------|
| **Situational** | What was happening at the time | Affects interpretation |
| **Emotional** | Tone, energy, sentiment | Changes meaning of words |
| **Temporal** | When in session, order of topics | Shows evolution of thinking |
| **Environmental** | Interruptions, setting factors | Explains participant behavior |
| **Relational** | How topics connect to each other | Enables sense-making |

**Example - Context Matters:**

**Without context:**
```
P: "It's fine, I guess"
```

**With context:**
```
P: "It's fine, I guess" [flat tone, long pause before responding, previous enthusiasm notably absent - seems disappointed but not saying so directly]
```

The context transforms interpretation.

#### 4.8.6 Timeliness Standards

**Documentation Timeliness:**

| Task | Standard Timeline | Why This Matters |
|------|-------------------|------------------|
| Save notes during session | Continuously (auto-save) | Prevent data loss |
| Initial cleanup | Within 1 hour of session | Memory still fresh |
| Session summary | Within 2 hours of session | Capture insights before they fade |
| Final notes | Within 24 hours of session | Enable immediate use for next sessions |
| Transcription request | Within 24 hours of session | Maintain study momentum |
| Note sharing with team | Within 24 hours of session | Enable collaborative learning |

**Why Speed Matters:**

- Memory fades exponentially (50% loss in 1 hour)
- Insights inform subsequent sessions
- Team members need access for collaboration
- Delays create bottlenecks in analysis

### 4.9 Special Considerations

#### 4.9.1 Remote vs. In-Person Sessions

**Remote Session Documentation:**

| Consideration | Documentation Approach |
|---------------|------------------------|
| **Screen recordings available** | Take lighter behavioral notes; focus on verbal; can review screen recording for detailed interaction |
| **Chat messages** | Save chat log; participants sometimes type comments |
| **Technical issues** | Document tech problems and their impact on session |
| **Reduced body language** | More reliance on verbal cues; note tone and energy |
| **Platform notes** | Some platforms (Zoom, Teams) have built-in note features |

**In-Person Session Documentation:**

| Consideration | Documentation Approach |
|---------------|------------------------|
| **Body language visible** | Document gestures, posture, facial expressions |
| **Physical interactions** | Note how participant interacts with objects, space |
| **Setting details** | Document environmental context |
| **Artifacts** | Capture photos of participant's materials, workspace (with permission) |
| **Side conversations** | Easier to capture informal comments before/after |

#### 4.9.2 Sensitive Topics Documentation

**When researching sensitive topics:**

| Consideration | Documentation Approach |
|---------------|------------------------|
| **Emotional moments** | Document with care and respect; note emotions without sensationalizing |
| **Trauma or distress** | Note support provided; document decision to pause or stop if needed |
| **Confidential information** | Extra care with anonymization; consider not documenting highly identifying details |
| **Participant discomfort** | Document level of comfort; affects validity of data |
| **Legal concerns** | Consult with Research Lead if participant discloses something legally problematic |

**Sensitive Documentation Example:**

```
✓ APPROPRIATE:
"Participant became emotional when discussing [topic]. Offered break;
participant chose to continue. Expressed frustration with [issue]."

✗ INAPPROPRIATE:
"Participant started crying and got really upset. It was uncomfortable."
```

**Ethical Documentation:**

- Respect participant dignity in how you describe them
- Focus on research-relevant information
- Avoid judgment or commentary
- Consult UXR-001 if participant discloses harm, abuse, illegal activity

#### 4.9.3 B2B and Industrial Research

**Industrial Research Documentation Considerations:**

| Aspect | Consideration | Documentation Approach |
|--------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Technical terminology** | Precise language matters | Capture exact terms; ask for spelling/definitions |
| **Complex workflows** | Hard to capture in real-time | Sketch diagrams; request copies of forms/documents; take photos |
| **Industry context** | Crucial for interpretation | Document organizational structure, regulations, standards mentioned |
| **Proprietary information** | May have NDA implications | Follow NDA guidelines; anonymize company-specific details |
| **Multiple stakeholders** | Different perspectives | Note participant's role and how it affects their perspective |

**Field Notes for Industrial Settings:**

```
CONTEXTUAL INQUIRY: Manufacturing Floor Process

ENVIRONMENT:
- Factory floor, 85°F, moderate noise level (~70dB)
- Operator station has computer terminal, phone, clipboard with forms
- 3 other operators visible performing similar tasks nearby

WORKFLOW OBSERVED:
[Time] [Action] [Tools Used] [Notes]

14:18  Enters data into system         Terminal     Uses printed reference card taped to desk [PHOTO 1]
14:20  System error message appears    Terminal     Checks notebook for fix [ARTIFACT 1]
...

ARTIFACTS CAPTURED:
- Photo 1: Reference card - handwritten codes and meanings
- Artifact 1: Personal notebook - 50+ pages of fixes and workarounds
- Photo 2: Production log form - official tracking document
```

#### 4.9.4 Multi-Language Sessions

**When sessions involve language differences:**

| Scenario | Documentation Approach |
|----------|------------------------|
| **Interpreter present** | Note both original language (if you catch key words) and interpretation; note interpretation delays |
| **Participant speaks non-native language** | Note language challenges; don't correct grammar in quotes; capture intended meaning |
| **Researcher speaks non-native language** | Extra care with transcription; may need native speaker to review |
| **Code-switching** | Note when participant switches languages; may indicate comfort level or topic sensitivity |

**Example Multi-Language Notes:**

```
P: "We use... how do you say... [speaks to colleague in Spanish]...
the system for tracking the orders"

[Note: Participant asked colleague for English word; more comfortable
discussing technical topics in Spanish]
```

#### 4.9.5 Accessibility Considerations

**When working with participants with disabilities:**

| Scenario | Documentation Consideration |
|----------|----------------------------|
| **Assistive technology use** | Document how participant uses AT; note barriers and successes |
| **Communication accommodations** | If sign language interpreter, note interpretation approach; if text-based, note communication pace |
| **Cognitive accessibility** | Note if guide adjustments were needed; session length modifications |
| **Physical accessibility** | Document environmental accommodations made |

**Respectful Documentation:**

```
✓ APPROPRIATE:
"Participant uses screen reader (JAWS). Successfully navigated to product
page. Screen reader did not announce button label, causing confusion."

✗ INAPPROPRIATE:
"Blind participant couldn't find the button."
```

**Focus on barriers, not person:**

- "The interface was not compatible with screen reader" (not "The participant couldn't use it")
- "Button labels were not descriptive enough for voice navigation" (barrier-focused)

### Role Definitions

**Research Lead:**

- Establishes documentation standards and best practices
- Trains team on effective note-taking techniques
- Reviews note quality and provides feedback
- Ensures compliance with data handling procedures (UXR-002)
- Maintains template library and updates standards
- Audits documentation completeness across studies

**Researcher (Lead/Moderator):**

- Takes primary responsibility for session documentation
- Takes notes during sessions (light notes if moderating; comprehensive if observing)
- Completes post-session summary within required timeframe
- Ensures notes are complete, accurate, and properly anonymized
- Manages transcription for their sessions
- Organizes and stores notes per standards
- Maintains research journal throughout study
- Shares documentation with team appropriately

**Researcher (Observer):**

- Takes detailed notes when observing (if not moderating)
- Focuses on assigned observation area if roles divided
- Contributes to post-session debrief
- Reviews and supplements primary notetaker's documentation
- Supports transcription review if needed

**Research Operations:**

- Manages transcription vendor relationships
- Maintains secure storage infrastructure
- Ensures backup procedures are working
- Provides technical support for note-taking tools
- Audits storage compliance with UXR-002
- Archives completed studies

**Stakeholder:**

- Receives session summaries as appropriate
- Provides feedback if they observe sessions
- Does not have access to raw notes with PII
- Consumes synthesized findings from documentation

## 7. Quality Checks

### 7.1 During-Session Quality Check

Monitor quality in real-time:

| Check | Standard | How to Monitor |
|-------|----------|----------------|
| **Capturing key quotes** | At least 3-5 notable quotes per session | Mark quotes as you capture them; use "QUOTE" tag |
| **Noting behaviors** | Behavioral observations beyond just words | Include tone, reactions, body language notes |
| **Timestamping critical moments** | Key moments have timestamps | Note time for important moments; mark for later review |
| **Maintaining pace** | Keeping up with conversation | If falling behind, switch to shorter paraphrase; focus on key info |
| **Coverage** | All major topics addressed | Check off guide sections as you cover them |

**Mid-Session Self-Check:**

At transition points, quickly assess:
- Am I capturing enough detail?
- Am I getting key quotes?
- Am I noting behaviors and context?
- Am I falling behind? Need to adjust approach?

## Appendix C: FAQ/Troubleshooting

**Q: How much should I write during sessions?**

A:
- Aim for comprehensive but sustainable
- Capture key quotes, main points, behaviors, context
- You don't need to transcribe everything; focus on what matters
- If you're moderating, take lighter notes; rely on recording or observer
- If you're observing, take detailed notes
- Balance: Enough detail to understand what happened without overwhelming yourself

**Q: What if I miss something important?**

A:
- Mark it in your notes: [MISSED - review recording at XX:XX]
- Use timestamps to find it in recording later
- Ask participant to repeat if critical and still in session
- Rely on recording to fill gaps after session
- Prevention: Have backup notetaker or recording

**Q: Should I take notes if I'm recording the session?**

A:
- **YES**, absolutely take notes even when recording
- Recording is backup, not replacement for notes
- Notes structure your thinking during session
- Notes capture what stood out to you
- Notes enable quick reference without rewatching hours of video
- You may never fully transcribe recording; notes remain primary documentation
- Notes capture your observations and interpretations that audio doesn't

**Q: How do I balance note-taking with moderating?**

A:
- **If you're the moderator, keep notes light:**
  - Focus on key quotes and moments
  - Mark sections of guide as you cover them
  - Jot down surprises and follow-ups
  - Note behavioral observations briefly
  - Use margins of guide for quick annotations
- **Rely on:**
  - Recording for verbatim content later
  - Observer to take detailed notes
  - Post-session summary to capture insights
- **Your priority as moderator:** Building rapport and following up insightfully matter more than comprehensive notes

**Q: What's the difference between notes and transcripts?**

A:
- **Notes:** Selective documentation; captures what matters; includes your observations; structured around guide; ready to use
- **Transcripts:** Complete record of words spoken; verbatim; no researcher interpretation; expensive and time-consuming
- **Use both when:** High-stakes research, formal analysis, verbatim quotes critical
- **Use notes only when:** Budget/time limited, clear notes sufficient, not every word needed
- **Transcripts don't replace notes:** Notes include context, observations, and structure that transcripts lack

**Q: How detailed should field notes be?**

A:
- Detailed enough to:
  - Understand what happened when you read them later
  - Transfer knowledge to someone who wasn't there
  - Support analysis and synthesis
  - Provide evidence for findings
- Balance:
  - Capture key observations during session (abbreviated OK)
  - Flesh out details immediately after while memory fresh
  - Prioritize observations that answer research questions
- Field notes are often MORE detailed than interview notes because:
  - You're observing process, not just asking questions
  - Environmental context is critical
  - Workflow sequence matters
  - Photos/artifacts need annotation

**Q: What if multiple observers have conflicting notes?**

A:
- **This is normal and valuable** - different perspectives enrich understanding
- **How to handle:**
  1. Acknowledge both perspectives
  2. Review recording if available to verify facts
  3. Discuss interpretation differences
  4. Document both if legitimately different ways to interpret
  5. Let analysis process reveal which interpretation has more support
- **Different ≠ Wrong:** One person might notice body language while another focuses on words
- **Consolidated notes should include diverse observations:** "[Observer 1] noted participant seemed excited; [Observer 2] noted some hesitation in response"

**Q: How long should post-session reflection take?**

A:
- **Immediate post-session tasks:** 30-60 minutes
  - Cleaning up notes: 15-30 min
  - Session summary: 15-20 min
  - File organization: 5-10 min
- **Research journal entry:** 10-20 minutes
  - Can be done after several sessions, not necessarily each one
- **Total time investment:**
  - 60-minute session: Budget 30-60 min post-session
  - 90-minute session: Budget 45-75 min post-session
- **Why this matters:**
  - Memory fades fast; time investment now saves re-work later
  - Good documentation is research efficiency, not overhead

**Q: What if I can't read my own handwriting?**

A:
- **Prevention:**
  - Write legibly enough for yourself during session
  - Transfer to digital within 1 hour while you can still decipher
  - Use printed guide with typed questions; only handwritten responses
  - Consider switching to digital notes if recurring problem
- **If it happens:**
  - Review recording to figure out what you wrote
  - Mark unclear sections: [Illegible - likely about X based on context]
  - Learn from it: Improve handwriting or switch to digital

**Q: How do I document something I don't understand?**

A:
- **During session:**
  - Ask for clarification: "Can you help me understand what you mean by [term]?"
  - Ask for example: "Can you give me an example of that?"
  - Ask them to explain: "Can you break that down for me?"
- **In notes:**
  - Document what was said even if unclear
  - Mark it: [UNCLEAR: Participant described X, possible meanings: Y or Z]
  - Note follow-up needed: ? Need to clarify this in future sessions
  - Don't pretend you understood when you didn't
- **After session:**
  - Discuss with team
  - Research unfamiliar terms
  - Probe more in next session
  - If participant was unclear vs. you not understanding, that's a finding

**Q: Should I document everything a participant says, even if it seems irrelevant?**

A:
- **During session:** Err on side of capturing more
  - What seems irrelevant might matter later
  - Context that seems tangential often provides insights
  - You can always ignore it later; you can't recover what you didn't capture
- **But also:** Focus on research questions
  - Don't transcribe long tangents unrelated to your topic
  - Capture gist of off-topic content: [Participant talked about X for 5 minutes - not relevant to study]
  - Redirect participant if veering too far off topic
- **Balance:** Comprehensive documentation of relevant content; summary of tangents

**Q: What if the session revealed something sensitive or concerning?**

A:
- **Document what happened:**
  - Capture the information professionally
  - Note your response and any support provided
  - Document decision to pause/stop if applicable
- **Follow protocol:**
  - If disclosure of harm/abuse/illegal activity, follow UXR-001 Section 4.8.1
  - Notify Research Lead immediately
  - Consider whether participant needs resources (but stay in scope of researcher role)
- **Anonymization:**
  - Extra care with highly sensitive information
  - Consider whether certain details need to be documented at all
  - Balance research value with participant privacy
- **Self-care:**
  - Debrief with Research Lead if session was difficult
  - Acknowledge impact on you
  - Seek support if needed

**Q: How do I know when my notes are "good enough"?**

A:
- **Good enough when:**
  - You can answer research questions from the notes
  - Someone else could understand what happened
  - Key quotes are captured verbatim
  - Behaviors and context are documented
  - Notes pass quality checklist (Appendix B)
  - You'd be comfortable using these notes for analysis in 6 months
- **Not good enough when:**
  - You can't remember what your abbreviations mean
  - Critical information is missing
  - You can't distinguish your observations from participant statements
  - Context is insufficient to interpret responses
  - You wouldn't be able to write a finding based on these notes alone
- **Test:** If you had to write the study findings using only these notes (no recording), could you? If yes, they're good enough.

## Appendix D: Documentation Troubleshooting Guide

**PROBLEM: Falling behind during fast-paced session**

**Solutions:**
- Switch to shorter paraphrasing instead of verbatim
- Use more abbreviations (have system ready)
- Focus on key points; mark moments to revisit in recording
- Tag important quotes with timestamp; transcribe later
- Slow participant down: "Let me make sure I capture that..."
- If observer is present, signal them to pick up note-taking

---

**PROBLEM: Participant speaks very technically and I don't understand**

**Solutions:**
- Ask for clarification in the moment: "Can you explain [term] for me?"
- Ask for simpler explanation: "How would you explain that to someone new to your field?"
- Document what they said even if unclear, mark [UNCLEAR]
- Look up terms after session
- Prepare better next time: Research domain terminology before sessions
- Have subject matter expert review notes

---

**PROBLEM: Moderating alone (no observer) and can't take good notes**

**Solutions:**
- Record session (with consent) and rely on recording
- Take lighter notes: Key quotes, surprises, behavioral notes only
- Use interview guide margins for quick annotations
- Do thorough post-session summary immediately after (within 1 hour)
- Consider adjusting session length to allow breaks for note-catch-up
- Mark timestamps for important moments to revisit
- For future: Advocate for having observer or recording

---

**PROBLEM: Notes feel scattered and unorganized**

**Solutions:**
- Use structured template (Section 5 Templates)
- Organize by interview guide sections
- Clean up and restructure immediately after session
- Use clear headers and separators
- During session: Mark which guide section you're on
- Try Cornell method (Section 4.3.2)
- Transfer handwritten notes to digital with structure

---

**PROBLEM: Captured lots of content but can't find key insights**

**Solutions:**
- Complete post-session summary (Template 5.4) - forces you to identify key points
- Re-read notes and tag/highlight key insights
- Create separate "Key Insights" section at top
- Use consistent tagging during session (!! for important)
- Make notes scannable: Headers, bullets, white space
- Create session summary with 3-5 main takeaways

---

**PROBLEM: Multiple sessions and notes are becoming overwhelming**

**Solutions:**
- Maintain research journal (Template 5.5) to synthesize across sessions
- Create session summaries (not just full notes) for quick reference
- Use consistent structure across all session notes
- Tag themes consistently across sessions
- Create master document with key quotes and themes
- Don't let notes pile up; document immediately after each session
- Consider using research platform (Dovetail) for better organization

---

**PROBLEM: Can't tell from my notes what participant said vs. what I thought**

**Solutions:**
- Use clear attribution: P: = participant, R: = researcher
- Use [brackets] for your observer comments
- Put participant quotes in "quotation marks"
- Label your reflections: [OBSERVER NOTE: ...]
- Go back through notes and clarify attribution
- Train yourself to mark it clearly as you write
- Review this issue in notes quality check

---

**PROBLEM: Team member's notes are inconsistent with mine**

**Solutions:**
- Normal to have different observations - both valuable
- Compare notes in debrief meeting
- Review recording if available to clarify facts
- Discuss different interpretations
- Consolidated notes can include multiple perspectives
- Agree on primary notetaker for future to ensure one consistent record
- Use this as learning opportunity about bias and perspective

---

**PROBLEM: Participant requested we not record, and now I'm worried about missing things**

**Solutions:**
- Take more detailed notes than usual during session
- Ask participant to slow down if needed: "I want to make sure I capture this accurately..."
- Write down exact quotes for key statements
- Consider having second observer to backup note-taking
- Post-session: Immediately clean up and expand notes while fresh
- Acknowledge in notes: [Recording declined; notes are primary documentation]

---

**PROBLEM: Realized after session that I forgot to document something important**

**Solutions:**
- Add it immediately to notes with timestamp: [Added post-session from memory: ...]
- Mark uncertainty if you're not 100% sure: [Believed participant said X, but not certain]
- Check recording if available
- Note in research journal as reminder for future sessions
- Don't add fabricated details; only what you genuinely remember
- Use as learning: What system would prevent this next time?

---

**PROBLEM: I'm spending more time documenting than analyzing**

**Solutions:**
- This is normal early in a study; documentation is essential
- As study progresses: Start ongoing analysis, not waiting until end (see UXR-007)
- Research journal helps turn documentation into early analysis
- Post-session summaries provide quick synthesis
- Documentation time decreases with practice and better systems
- Consider: Are you over-documenting? Focus on research questions
- Balance: Comprehensive documentation is investment that makes analysis rigorous
