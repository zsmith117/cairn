## 1. Purpose

This Standard Operating Procedure establishes the requirements and procedures for analyzing qualitative research data and synthesizing findings. It ensures that:

- Analysis is rigorous, systematic, and defensible
- Findings are grounded in evidence from research data
- Insights are meaningful, actionable, and relevant to research questions
- Analysis processes are transparent and reproducible
- Multiple perspectives are integrated during synthesis
- Patterns and themes are identified consistently
- Findings are validated before being communicated
- Analysis quality is maintained across different researchers and studies
- Research outputs provide clear value to stakeholders
- The path from data to insights to recommendations is traceable

**Why This Matters:**

The quality of analysis directly determines the value of research. Poor analysis leads to:
- Insights that don't actually reflect participant experiences
- Missing critical patterns in the data
- Findings that confirm researcher bias rather than reveal truth
- Recommendations that aren't grounded in evidence
- Inability to defend findings when challenged
- Stakeholder skepticism about research rigor
- Wasted research investment if insights don't drive decisions
- Lost opportunity to learn from rich qualitative data
- Cherry-picking data to support predetermined conclusions
- Insights that are too vague or generic to be actionable

Rigorous analysis transforms raw research data into credible insights that drive better decisions and outcomes.

**This SOP applies to:**

- Thematic analysis of interview data
- Usability testing findings synthesis

- Focus group analysis
- Concept testing evaluation
- Cross-study synthesis (meta-analysis of multiple studies)
- Journey mapping and service design research
- Evaluative and generative research analysis

- Analysis planning and preparation

- Coding and categorization approaches

- Affinity mapping and collaborative synthesis
- Framework and model development

## 3. Definitions

| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Analysis** | Systematic examination of research data to identify patterns, meanings, and relationships |
| **Synthesis** | Integration of findings across multiple data sources or sessions into coherent insights |
| **Coding** | Process of labeling segments of data with descriptive or interpretive tags |
| **Theme** | Recurring pattern of meaning that captures something important about the data in relation to research questions |
| **Pattern** | Observable regularity or consistency in data across participants or sessions |
| **Insight** | Meaningful interpretation that explains why patterns exist and what they mean; goes beyond observation |
| **Finding** | Evidence-based statement about what the research discovered; supported by data |
| **Observation** | Factual statement about what was seen or heard during research; lowest level of abstraction |
| **Recommendation** | Actionable suggestion based on insights; highest level of abstraction |
| **Affinity Mapping** | Collaborative technique for organizing qualitative data by grouping related observations into themes |
| **Open Coding** | Initial coding pass where codes emerge from data without predetermined categories |
| **Axial Coding** | Second coding pass that relates codes to each other and identifies relationships |
| **Thematic Analysis** | Method for identifying, analyzing, and reporting patterns (themes) within qualitative data |
| **Code Saturation** | Point where no new codes or themes emerge from additional data |
| **Inter-rater Reliability** | Degree of agreement between multiple coders; indicates consistency |
| **Negative Evidence** | Data that contradicts emerging patterns or themes; must be considered |
| **Disconfirming Evidence** | Evidence that challenges or refutes an interpretation; strengthens validity when acknowledged |
| **Grounded Theory** | Approach where theories emerge from data rather than being imposed on it |
| **Inductive Analysis** | Analysis approach where patterns emerge from data (bottom-up) |
| **Deductive Analysis** | Analysis approach using predetermined framework (top-down) |
| **Abductive Analysis** | Iterative approach combining inductive pattern-finding with deductive framework-testing |

## 4. Procedure

### 4.1 Planning for Analysis

#### 4.1.1 Define Analysis Approach

Before beginning analysis, establish approach based on research goals:

**Analysis Approach Decision Framework:**

| Research Goal | Recommended Approach | Analysis Focus |
|---------------|---------------------|----------------|
| Understand user needs, pain points, motivations | **Thematic Analysis (Inductive)** | Identify patterns and themes emerging from data |
| Evaluate against known framework (e.g., usability heuristics) | **Deductive Analysis** | Map findings to predetermined categories |
| Develop new model or framework | **Grounded Theory** | Build theory from data; let structure emerge |
| Compare experiences across user segments | **Comparative Analysis** | Identify similarities and differences between groups |
| Assess single product/feature | **Focused Synthesis** | Organize findings by research questions |
| Build on prior research | **Abductive Analysis** | Test and refine existing frameworks with new data |

**Most Common for UX Research:**

- **Thematic Analysis (Inductive)** - Start with data, let themes emerge
- Use for: Generative research, exploratory interviews, discovery

**When to Choose:**

| Factor | Inductive (Emergent) | Deductive (Framework-Based) |
|--------|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Research maturity | Early exploration | Well-understood domain |
| Goal | Discover the unexpected | Evaluate against known criteria |
| Flexibility | High - open to surprises | Lower - focused lens |
| Framework exists | No | Yes |
| Example | "What are users' pain points?" | "How well does product meet usability heuristics?" |

#### 4.1.2 Review Research Questions

Ground analysis in original research questions:

```
ANALYSIS PLANNING TEMPLATE

STUDY: [Study name]
RESEARCH QUESTIONS:
1. [Primary research question]
2. [Secondary research question]
3. [Additional questions]

ANALYSIS APPROACH: [Inductive Thematic Analysis / Deductive / etc.]

ANALYSIS SCOPE:
- Data to analyze: [N sessions, N transcripts, N pages of notes]
- Timeline: [Start date] to [End date]
- Team members: [Who's involved]

SUCCESS CRITERIA:
- Answer all research questions with evidence
- Identify key themes and patterns
- Provide actionable insights
- Deliver by: [Date]

DELIVERABLES:
[ ] Themes codebook
[ ] Key findings document
[ ] Evidence repository
[ ] Final report / presentation
```

#### 4.1.3 Assess Data Readiness

**Pre-Analysis Checklist:**

```
DATA READINESS FOR ANALYSIS

DOCUMENTATION COMPLETE:
[ ] All session notes finalized per UXR-006
[ ] Session summaries completed
[ ] Transcripts completed (if using)
[ ] Research journal reviewed
[ ] All files properly stored and accessible

DATA QUALITY:
[ ] Notes are readable and complete
[ ] Key quotes captured verbatim
[ ] Context preserved in notes
[ ] Participant attribution clear
[ ] Anonymization complete per UXR-002

TEAM READINESS:
[ ] Analysis roles defined (who's doing what)
[ ] Analysis timeline established
[ ] Workspace prepared (physical or digital)
[ ] Tools selected and ready
[ ] Analysis sessions scheduled

READY TO BEGIN: [ ] Yes [ ] No - needs: _______________
```

**If Data Not Ready:**

- Complete missing documentation immediately
- Don't begin formal analysis with incomplete data
- Risk: Biasing analysis toward sessions analyzed first

### 4.2 Data Familiarization and Immersion

#### 4.2.1 Initial Data Immersion

Before formal coding, immerse yourself in the data:

**Immersion Process:**

| Step | Activity | Purpose | Time Investment |
|------|----------|---------|-----------------|
| 1. Read all notes | Read through all session notes without coding | Get holistic view; sense of whole dataset | 2-4 hours for 8-10 sessions |
| 2. Review session summaries | Read all post-session summaries | Identify recurring themes mentioned across sessions | 30-60 min |
| 3. Read research journal | Review patterns noted during data collection | Surface preliminary hypotheses | 15-30 min |
| 4. Listen to key moments | If recorded, listen to important segments | Hear tone, emotion, context | 1-2 hours |
| 5. Note initial impressions | Write down first impressions and patterns | Document intuitions before formal analysis | 30 min |

**Initial Impressions Document:**

```
INITIAL ANALYSIS IMPRESSIONS

Date: [Date]
Analyst: [Name]
Data reviewed: [N sessions]

OVERALL IMPRESSIONS:
- What struck me most:
- Surprising findings:
- Expected findings confirmed:

PRELIMINARY PATTERNS (before formal coding):
1. [Pattern you're noticing]
   - Evidence: Sessions/participants where seen
   - Confidence: High / Medium / Low

2. [Another pattern]
   - Evidence:
   - Confidence:

QUESTIONS RAISED:
- [Questions the data raises]

POTENTIAL THEMES TO EXPLORE:
- [Areas that seem significant]

[These are hypotheses to test during formal analysis]
```

**Why Immersion Matters:**

- Prevents premature coding that misses context
- Allows patterns to emerge naturally
- Builds intuition about the data
- Identifies areas needing deeper attention
- Provides quality check on data completeness

#### 4.2.2 Identify Analysis Units

Determine what you'll be analyzing:

**Analysis Unit Options:**

| Unit | Description | When to Use | Example |
|------|-------------|-------------|---------|
| **Utterance** | Single statement by participant | Detailed linguistic analysis | "It's frustrating" = 1 unit |
| **Exchange** | Question + response | Understanding specific interactions | Q: "How often?" A: "Daily" = 1 unit |
| **Topic segment** | All discussion on one topic | Standard thematic analysis | Entire discussion about Feature X = 1 unit |
| **Session** | Entire session as one unit | High-level pattern analysis | Each participant = 1 case |
| **Quote** | Meaningful verbatim statement | Quote-based analysis | Flagged quotes in notes |

**Recommended for Most UX Research:**

- **Topic segment** - Allows rich thematic analysis while maintaining context
- Capture participant's complete thoughts on a topic
- Preserves context and meaning

### 4.3 Coding Approaches

#### 4.3.1 Open Coding (First Pass)

**Open Coding Process:**

Start with fresh perspective; let codes emerge from data.

**How to Open Code:**

1. **Read segment of data** (e.g., response to one question)
2. **Ask:** What is this about? What is the participant saying/experiencing?
3. **Assign descriptive code(s)** - Can be:
   - Descriptive: Labels what's there ("uses workaround")
   - Interpretive: Captures meaning ("frustrated with process")
   - In-vivo: Uses participant's own words ("'nail jello to wall'")
4. **Move to next segment**, repeat

**Coding Example:**

```
SESSION NOTES - Segment:

P: "I have to export the data to Excel every day because the system
doesn't have the report I need. It takes about 30 minutes. I've been
doing this for two years. I've asked IT multiple times for a better
solution but they say it's not a priority."

CODES APPLIED:
- Manual workaround [DESCRIPTIVE]
- Time waste / inefficiency [INTERPRETIVE]
- Unmet need - reporting [DESCRIPTIVE]
- Repetitive task [DESCRIPTIVE]
- IT bottleneck [DESCRIPTIVE]
- Deprioritized user need [INTERPRETIVE]
- Resignation / learned helplessness [INTERPRETIVE]

[Multiple codes per segment is normal]
```

**Open Coding Principles:**

| Principle | What It Means | Example |
|-----------|---------------|---------|
| **Stay close to data** | Use participant language when possible | Code as "doesn't trust data" not "data quality issue" |
| **Code generously early** | Over-code initially; refine later | Multiple codes per segment OK |
| **Question assumptions** | Don't impose your interpretations | Check: Am I hearing them or my expectations? |
| **Descriptive first** | Start with what's there before interpreting | "uses Excel" before "frustrated with tools" |
| **Note context** | Preserve context in code or memo | "frustrated" needs context: about what? |

**Code Creation Guidelines:**

```
GOOD CODES:
✓ "Manual workaround"
✓ "Time pressure"
✓ "Lacks training"
✓ "Relies on colleague knowledge"

CODES NEEDING IMPROVEMENT:
✗ "Problem" - too vague, with what?
✗ "Good" - not descriptive
✗ "Fix this" - not analytical
✗ "User error" - potentially judgmental
```

#### 4.3.2 Code Organization and Management

**Codebook Structure:**

Create codebook to track codes:

```
CODEBOOK - [Study Name]

Last updated: [Date]
Version: [X.X]

---

CODE: Manual workaround
DEFINITION: Participant has created informal process to compensate for
            system limitation
WHEN TO USE: When participant describes adaptation or "hack"
EXAMPLES: - Exporting to Excel for analysis
          - Keeping personal notes because system doesn't track
          - Using personal system in parallel
RELATED CODES: Unmet need, System limitation, Inefficiency

---

CODE: Time pressure
DEFINITION: Participant experiences time constraints that affect workflow
WHEN TO USE: When time is mentioned as factor affecting decisions or satisfaction
EXAMPLES: - "I don't have time to..."
          - "We're always rushing"
          - "Deadline pressure makes me skip steps"
RELATED CODES: Workflow inefficiency, Shortcuts taken, Quality compromised

---

[Continue for all codes...]

---

CODE CATEGORIES (emerging):

WORKFLOW & PROCESS:
- Manual workaround
- Time pressure
- Inefficiency
- Interruptions

- Unmet need - reporting
- Unmet need - automation
- Unmet need - training

SYSTEM ISSUES:
- System limitation
- Error / bug
- Performance problem

[Categories emerge during analysis]
```

**Digital Codebook Tools:**

| Tool | Best For | Features |
|------|----------|----------|
| Spreadsheet (Excel, Google Sheets) | Simple studies, small teams | Sortable, filterable, easy to share |
| Airtable | Medium complexity | Linked records, views, collaboration |
| Notion | Rich documentation | Linked databases, formatting |
| Dedicated software (NVivo, Dedoose, Atlas.ti) | Large studies, academic rigor | Advanced features, inter-rater reliability |

**Codebook Evolution:**

Codebooks are living documents:

| Phase | Activity | Version |
|-------|----------|---------|
| Initial | After coding 2-3 sessions | v0.1 - Draft |
| Refinement | After coding half the data | v1.0 - Working |
| Stabilization | After coding all data | v2.0 - Final |
| Synthesis | After theme development | v2.1 - With theme mapping |

#### 4.3.3 Axial Coding (Second Pass)

After open coding, identify relationships between codes:

**Axial Coding Questions:**

- Which codes appear together frequently?
- Which codes are related? How?
- Which codes are causes vs. effects?
- Which codes are context vs. action vs. outcome?
- What patterns connect multiple codes?

**Relationship Mapping:**

```
AXIAL CODING - CODE RELATIONSHIPS

PATTERN: Manual Workarounds

CAUSAL STRUCTURE:

CONTEXT:
- System limitation [code]
- Unmet need [code]

LEADS TO:

ACTION:
- Manual workaround [code]
- Uses alternative tool [code]

RESULTS IN:

CONSEQUENCES:
- Time inefficiency [code]
- Frustration [code]
- Risk of error [code]

MODERATING FACTORS:
- IT prioritization [code]
- Budget constraints [code]
```

**Creating Code Clusters:**

Group related codes into meaningful clusters:

```
CODE CLUSTER: "Compensating for System Limitations"

CODES IN THIS CLUSTER:
- Manual workaround (12 instances)
- Uses Excel for analysis (8 instances)
- Maintains personal tracker (6 instances)
- Asks colleague instead of system (9 instances)

UNIFYING THEME:
Users actively adapt to fill gaps in system functionality

SIGNIFICANCE:
High effort spent on workarounds; indicates priority areas for improvement
```

#### 4.3.4 Coding Quality and Consistency

**Self-Check Questions:**

While coding, regularly ask:

| Question | Purpose |
|----------|---------|
| Am I applying codes consistently? | Check reliability |
| Am I creating too many similar codes? | May need consolidation |
| Are my codes too specific or too broad? | Check granularity |
| Can I define what each code means? | Check clarity |
| Would someone else code this the same way? | Check objectivity |

**Inter-Rater Reliability (If Team Coding):**

When multiple people code:

1. **Independent coding** - Each person codes same 2-3 sessions independently
2. **Compare** - Calculate agreement rate
3. **Discuss disagreements** - Calibrate understanding
4. **Refine codebook** - Clarify definitions
5. **Re-code if needed** - If major inconsistencies
6. **Proceed** - Once adequate agreement reached

**Agreement Calculation:**

```
Agreement Rate = (Number of agreed codes) / (Total codes applied)

Target: >80% agreement for research-quality coding

If below 80%:
- Discuss: Why differences?
- Clarify: Code definitions need refinement?
- Re-code: Problematic sessions after calibration
```

### 4.4 Pattern and Theme Identification

#### 4.4.1 From Codes to Patterns

**Pattern Identification Process:**

| Step | Activity | Output |
|------|----------|--------|
| 1. Code frequency | Count how often each code appears | Code frequency list |
| 2. Code co-occurrence | Identify codes that appear together | Code relationship map |
| 3. Participant distribution | Note which participants/segments show each code | Pattern prevalence |
| 4. Contextual analysis | Understand conditions when codes appear | Pattern context |
| 5. Pattern naming | Name the pattern you're seeing | Descriptive pattern name |

**Pattern Template:**

```
PATTERN: [Descriptive name]

DESCRIPTION:
[What is this pattern? What recurring phenomenon are you seeing?]

EVIDENCE:
- Appears in: [N] of [Total] sessions
- Codes involved: [List related codes]
- Participant quotes:
  - "[Example quote 1]" - P[#]
  - "[Example quote 2]" - P[#]

CONTEXT:
- When does this pattern appear?
- What conditions make it more/less likely?

SIGNIFICANCE:
- Why does this matter?
- What does it tell us about user experience?

VARIATIONS:
- How does this pattern differ across participant segments?

RELATED PATTERNS:
- [Other patterns this connects to]
```

**Pattern Strength Assessment:**

| Strength | Criteria | Confidence |
|----------|----------|------------|
| **Strong** | Seen in >75% of sessions; consistent expression; clear evidence | High - likely generalizable |
| **Moderate** | Seen in 50-75% of sessions; some variation in expression | Medium - explore boundaries |
| **Emerging** | Seen in 25-50% of sessions; needs more data | Low-Medium - tentative |
| **Weak/Isolated** | Seen in <25% of sessions | Low - may be individual case |

#### 4.4.2 Theme Development

**Themes vs. Patterns:**

| Characteristic | Pattern | Theme |
|----------------|---------|-------|
| **Abstraction** | Lower | Higher |
| **Description** | What we observe in data | What it means; interpretive |
| **Example** | "Users create manual workarounds" | "System gaps force user innovation" |
| **Level** | Descriptive | Interpretive |

**Theme Development Process:**

1. **Review patterns** identified in previous step
2. **Ask:** What deeper meaning connects these patterns?
3. **Develop theme statement** that captures meaning
4. **Test theme** against data - does it hold?
5. **Refine** theme based on fit with data

**Theme Quality Criteria:**

| Criterion | Good Theme | Poor Theme |
|-----------|------------|------------|
| **Distinct** | Clearly different from other themes | Overlaps heavily with others |
| **Coherent** | Internal consistency; makes sense | Captures unrelated ideas |
| **Evidence-based** | Grounded in data with examples | Based on analyst assumptions |
| **Meaningful** | Says something important | Trivial or obvious |
| **Relevant** | Addresses research questions | Interesting but off-topic |

**Theme Template:**

```
THEME: [Theme name - should be evocative and memorable]

THEME STATEMENT:
[1-2 sentence description of what this theme captures]

SUPPORTING PATTERNS:
1. [Pattern name]
   - Evidence: [Brief summary]
2. [Pattern name]
   - Evidence: [Brief summary]
3. [Pattern name]
   - Evidence: [Brief summary]

SUPPORTING DATA:
Prevalence: Seen in [N] of [Total] participants
Key quotes:
- "[Representative quote that captures theme]" - P[#]
- "[Another strong quote]" - P[#]
- "[Third quote]" - P[#]

NARRATIVE DESCRIPTION:
[2-3 paragraphs explaining this theme in detail. Tell the story.
What is happening? Why? What does it mean for users? What are the
implications?]

VARIATIONS:
[How does this theme manifest differently across user segments,
contexts, or conditions?]

CONTRADICTORY EVIDENCE:
[Any data that doesn't fit this theme? Important to acknowledge.]

IMPLICATIONS:
[What does this theme mean for design/product/strategy?]

RELATED THEMES:
[How does this theme connect to other themes?]
```

Create visual representation of themes and relationships:

```
THEMATIC MAP

STUDY: [Name]
RESEARCH QUESTION: [Primary question]

===================================================================

                    OVERARCHING THEME
                  "System-User Adaptation Cycle"
                            |
          __________________|__________________
         |                  |                  |
         |                  |                  |
    THEME 1            THEME 2            THEME 3

| Validate | Share with team for feedback | Ensure shared understanding |
| Finalize | Create polished version | Use in reporting |

### 4.5 Affinity Mapping and Collaborative Synthesis

#### 4.5.1 Affinity Mapping Process

Collaborative technique for organizing qualitative data:

**Affinity Mapping Session Structure:**

```
AFFINITY MAPPING SESSION PLAN

STUDY: [Name]
DATE: [Date]
DURATION: 2-4 hours
PARTICIPANTS: [Research team + key stakeholders (optional)]
FACILITATOR: [Name]

MATERIALS NEEDED:
- Sticky notes (multiple colors)
- Sharpies
- Large wall space or digital board (Miro/Mural)
- Session notes (printed or accessible)
- Snacks and breaks planned

===================================================================

AGENDA:

1. SETUP (15 min)
   - Introduce study and research questions
   - Explain affinity mapping process
   - Review ground rules

2. INDIVIDUAL OBSERVATION CAPTURE (45-60 min)
   - Each person reviews data
   - Writes observations on sticky notes (1 observation per note)
   - Focus on: behaviors, quotes, pain points, needs, surprises
   - Aim for 50-100 notes per person

3. SILENT POSTING (15 min)
   - All participants post notes on wall simultaneously
   - Read others' notes as you post
   - No discussion yet

4. SILENT GROUPING (30-45 min)
   - Team moves notes into groups of related observations
   - No talking - communicate through moving notes
   - OK to move someone else's note
   - OK to create multiple groups
   - If note doesn't fit anywhere, leave it alone (parking lot)

5. GROUP DISCUSSION & LABELING (45-60 min)
   - Now you can talk!
   - Name each group - what theme unifies these?
   - Discuss surprising groups or patterns
   - Challenge: "Does this note really belong here?"
   - Identify super-groups (themes of themes)

6. REFINEMENT (30 min)
   - Refine group names
   - Order groups by importance/frequency
   - Identify relationships between groups
   - Flag contradictions or gaps

7. DOCUMENTATION (15 min)
   - Photograph final map
   - Assign someone to document digitally
   - Note key decisions and questions

===================================================================

OUTPUT:
- Affinity map (photo + digital documentation)
- List of themes with supporting observations
- Relationships between themes
- Questions for further exploration
```

**Affinity Mapping Ground Rules:**

| Rule | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| One observation per sticky note | Keeps units atomic and moveable |
| Use participant quotes when possible | Stays grounded in data |
| Capture observations, not interpretations (yet) | Analysis comes after grouping |
| Silent grouping before discussion | Prevents dominant voices; lets patterns emerge organically |
| Anyone can move any note | Collective intelligence; challenge assumptions |
| No "other" or "miscellaneous" groups | Forces meaningful categorization |

**Digital Affinity Mapping:**

| Tool | Best For | Features |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Miro** | Remote teams | Infinite canvas, templates, voting, timer |
| **Mural** | Remote teams | Similar to Miro, strong facilitation tools |
| **FigJam** | Design teams | Integrated with Figma, simple interface |
| **Physical wall** | Co-located teams | Tactile, collaborative, highly visible |

#### 4.5.2 Collaborative Analysis Sessions

**Multi-Session Analysis Approach:**

When dataset is large, break into multiple sessions:

| Session | Focus | Duration | Participants |
|---------|-------|----------|--------------|
| **Session 1: Immersion** | Review data together; initial impressions | 1-2 hours | Core research team |
| **Session 2: Initial Grouping** | Affinity mapping of observations | 2-3 hours | Research team + 1-2 stakeholders |
| **Session 3: Theme Development** | From groups to themes; narrative building | 2 hours | Core research team |
| **Session 4: Validation** | Test themes against data; refine | 1-2 hours | Core research team |
| **Session 5: Synthesis** | Create framework; develop recommendations | 2 hours | Research team + stakeholders |

**Team Roles in Collaborative Analysis:**

| Role | Responsibilities |
|------|------------------|
| **Facilitator** | Guides process; keeps team on track; manages time |
| **Scribe** | Documents decisions; photographs maps; takes notes |
| **Skeptic** | Challenges interpretations; asks "Where's the evidence?" |
| **Synthesizer** | Looks for connections; builds narrative |
| **Everyone** | Engages actively; shares perspectives; challenges assumptions |

#### 4.5.3 Handling Disagreements

**When team members disagree on interpretation:**

| Disagreement Type | Resolution Approach |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Factual** | Return to data; review notes or recording |
| **Interpretive** | Both views may be valid; document multiple interpretations |
| **Relevance** | Assess against research questions; prioritize accordingly |
| **Grouping** | Try both ways; which tells clearer story? |
| **Importance** | Use frequency and impact to decide; consider stakeholder input |

**Productive Disagreement Protocol:**

1. **Pause discussion** - "We have different views here"
2. **Articulate both perspectives** - Each person states their interpretation
3. **Identify source of disagreement** - Factual vs. interpretive?
4. **Review evidence** - Look at data together
5. **Test interpretations** - Which better fits full dataset?
6. **Document decision** - Why this interpretation was chosen
7. **Note alternative** - Keep other interpretation visible as possibility

### 4.6 From Data to Insights to Recommendations

#### 4.6.1 The Analysis Pyramid

**Levels of Abstraction:**

```
                    RECOMMENDATIONS
                   (What to do about it)
                   "Enable X to improve Y"
                          ▲
                          |
                      INSIGHTS
                   (Why it matters)
              "Users do X because Y, which means Z"
                          ▲
                          |
                       FINDINGS
                  (What we learned)
              "X% of users experience Y"
                          ▲
                          |
                    OBSERVATIONS
                   (What we saw/heard)
              "Participant said/did X"
                          ▲
                          |
                      RAW DATA
                (Notes, quotes, recordings)
```

**Moving Up the Pyramid:**

| Level | Question to Ask | Example |
|-------|-----------------|---------|
| **Observation** | What did I see/hear? | "Participant exports data to Excel daily" |
| **Finding** | What pattern exists? | "8 of 10 participants use Excel workarounds for reporting" |
| **Insight** | Why does this matter? What does it mean? | "Current reporting is inadequate for users' analytical needs, forcing time-consuming manual work. This suggests opportunities in custom reporting." |
| **Recommendation** | What should we do? | "Develop customizable reporting dashboard that enables ad-hoc analysis without export" |

**Insight Quality Criteria:**

| Criterion | Good Insight | Not Quite Insight |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Explanatory** | Explains why patterns exist | Only describes what exists |
| **Actionable** | Suggests direction for action | Too abstract to act on |
| **Evidence-based** | Grounded in data | Speculation or assumption |
| **Non-obvious** | Reveals something new | States the obvious |
| **Connected** | Links multiple data points | Based on single observation |

  - "[Quote]" - P[#]

```

#### 4.6.2 Developing Recommendations

**Recommendation Framework:**

Good recommendations are:

| Characteristic | Description | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Actionable** | Clear what to do | ✓ "Add export to CSV button on reports page"<br>✗ "Improve reporting" |
| **Grounded** | Based on insights from research | Cite the insight that led to this recommendation |
| **Specific** | Concrete, not vague | ✓ "Enable users to save custom report templates"<br>✗ "Make it easier" |
| **Prioritized** | Importance indicated | High/Medium/Low or Must/Should/Could |
| **Feasible** | Realistic to implement | Consider constraints, but don't self-censor too much |

**Recommendation Template:**

```
RECOMMENDATION: [Clear, actionable statement]

[How this will improve user experience]

- Impact: [Description of pain point being addressed]

  - "[Supporting quote]" - P[#]

[What to consider when implementing]
- Complexity: [High/Medium/Low]
- Dependencies: [What else is needed]
- Risks: [Potential downsides to consider]

SUCCESS METRICS:
[How you'd know if this recommendation worked]
- [Metric 1]
- [Metric 2]
```

**Prioritization Framework:**

| Priority | Criteria | Example |
|----------|----------|---------|
| **High** | - Affects majority of users<br>- Significant pain or opportunity<br>- Aligns with business goals<br>- Feasible to implement | Fix critical usability blocker preventing task completion |
| **Medium** | - Affects substantial subset<br>- Moderate pain or opportunity<br>- Supports business goals<br>- Implementation complexity moderate | Add feature to streamline frequent workflow |
| **Low** | - Affects small subset<br>- Minor pain or nice-to-have<br>- Indirect business value<br>- Complex implementation | Polish UI detail for edge case |

**Balancing User Needs and Business Goals:**

Recommendations should consider:
- **User needs** (from research)
- **Business goals** (from stakeholders)
- **Technical feasibility** (from engineering)
- **Resource constraints** (from reality)

Document when recommendations serve multiple objectives.

### 4.7 Validation and Quality Checks

#### 4.7.1 Validating Findings

**Validation Approaches:**

| Method | Description | When to Use |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Negative case analysis** | Actively search for data that contradicts findings | Always - strengthens validity |
| **Peer debriefing** | Discuss findings with colleague not involved | Medium-high stakes research |
| **Member checking** | Share findings with participants for feedback | Academic research; high stakes |
| **Triangulation** | Compare findings across data sources | When multiple data types available |
| **Audit trail** | Document analysis process for transparency | Formal research; compliance needs |

**Negative Case Analysis:**

Actively look for contradictions:

```
FINDING: Users prefer Feature A over Feature B

NEGATIVE CASE ANALYSIS:

Search for counter-examples:
- Did any participants prefer Feature B? → P3 did
- Under what conditions? → P3 is power user; uses advanced features in B
- How does this affect finding? → Refine: "Most users (8/10) prefer
  Feature A for basic tasks; power users may prefer Feature B for
  advanced capabilities"

REVISED FINDING:
Users prefer Feature A for common tasks due to simplicity; power users
may prefer Feature B when needing advanced features. Design should
accommodate both user types.
```

**Benefits of Negative Case Analysis:**

- Prevents overgeneralization
- Reveals important nuances
- Increases credibility
- Identifies boundary conditions

#### 4.7.2 Checking for Bias

**Common Analysis Biases:**

| Bias | Description | Mitigation |
|------|-------------|------------|
| **Confirmation bias** | Seeing only data that confirms expectations | Actively search for disconfirming evidence |
| **Availability bias** | Over-weighting recent or memorable sessions | Review all data systematically |
| **Anchoring bias** | Over-relying on first impressions | Revisit early interpretations after seeing all data |
| **Groupthink** | Team converges too quickly on interpretation | Assign someone to play devil's advocate |
| **Cherry-picking** | Selecting only data that supports preferred narrative | Use systematic coding; track all data |

**Bias Check Questions:**

```
ANALYSIS BIAS CHECK

1. CONFIRMATION BIAS:
   - What did I expect to find?
   - Did I find it? (If yes, extra scrutiny needed)
   - What data contradicts my expectations?
   - Am I giving equal attention to contradictory data?

2. STAKEHOLDER INFLUENCE:
   - What do stakeholders want to hear?
   - Am I unconsciously shaping findings to please them?
   - What would I conclude if stakeholders had different preferences?

3. RECENCY BIAS:
   - Which sessions am I remembering most clearly?
   - Am I over-weighting those sessions?
   - What did early sessions show that I might be forgetting?

4. PERSONAL EXPERIENCE:
   - How does my personal experience affect interpretation?
   - Am I projecting my frustrations/preferences onto users?
   - What would someone with different background conclude?

5. CHERRY-PICKING:
   - Can I support this finding across multiple participants?
   - Am I relying too heavily on one memorable quote?
   - What's the actual frequency vs. my perception?

ACTIONS TAKEN TO MITIGATE:
- [List specific steps taken to address biases identified]
```

#### 4.9.4 Multi-Study Synthesis

6. **Synthesize** - What can we confidently say across studies?
7. **Identify gaps** - What still needs research?

## Appendix B: Common Analysis Pitfalls

**PITFALL 1: Premature Conclusion**

❌ Problem: Deciding what the data means before analyzing it

✓ Solution:
- Document expectations before analysis
- Challenge conclusions that match expectations
- Seek disconfirming evidence
- Use systematic coding process

---

**PITFALL 2: Cherry-Picking Data**

❌ Problem: Selecting only data that supports preferred narrative

✓ Solution:
- Code all data, not just highlights
- Count frequencies
- Document prevalence
- Include contradictory evidence in findings

---

**PITFALL 3: Mistaking Observation for Insight**

❌ Bad: "Users export data to Excel" (observation)

✓ Good: "Users export to Excel because built-in reporting doesn't support their analytical workflow, revealing opportunity for custom reporting features" (insight)

---

**PITFALL 4: Over-Interpreting Limited Data**

❌ Problem: Making broad claims from one or two participants

✓ Solution:
- Note prevalence: "2 of 10 participants mentioned..."
- Use cautious language: "Some users..." not "Users..."
- Flag as emerging pattern needing more data

---

**PITFALL 5: Letting Stakeholders Drive Analysis**

❌ Problem: Analyzing to confirm what stakeholders want to hear

✓ Solution:
- Maintain research independence
- Report what data shows, not what's desired
- Include findings stakeholders may not like
- Be researcher first, pleaser second

---

**PITFALL 6: Analysis Paralysis**

❌ Problem: Over-analyzing; unable to reach conclusions

✓ Solution:
- Set deadlines for analysis phases
- Use "good enough" standard, not perfect
- Document uncertainty and move forward
- Recognize when more analysis won't improve findings

---

**PITFALL 7: Losing the Forest for the Trees**

❌ Problem: Getting lost in detail; missing big picture

✓ Solution:
- Regularly step back: "What's the story here?"
- Review research questions frequently
- Create thematic map for high-level view
- Discuss with team for fresh perspective

---

**PITFALL 8: Assuming Causation**

❌ Bad: "Feature X causes users to churn" (correlation ≠ causation)

✓ Good: "Users who churned mentioned frustration with Feature X; further investigation needed to understand causal relationship"

---

**PITFALL 9: Treating All Data as Equal Weight**

❌ Problem: Giving equal weight to strong patterns and outliers

✓ Solution:
- Document frequency/prevalence
- Note confidence level
- Distinguish strong patterns from weak signals
- Prioritize accordingly

---

**PITFALL 10: Skipping Validation**

❌ Problem: Moving straight from analysis to communication

✓ Solution:
- Always validate findings before sharing
- Negative case analysis
- Peer review
- Check bias
- Trace findings back to data

## Appendix C: FAQ

**Q: How long should analysis take?**

A:
- **Rule of thumb:** 1-2x the time spent in data collection
- **For 8-10 hour-long sessions:** 10-15 days of analysis
- **Breakdown:**
  - Coding: 40-50% of time
  - Theme development: 25-30%
  - Insight synthesis: 15-20%
  - Validation: 10-15%
- **Factors affecting time:**
  - Data volume
  - Complexity of research questions
  - Team vs. solo analysis
  - Experience level

**Q: Should I start analysis during data collection or wait until the end?**

A:
- **Best practice:** Ongoing analysis during collection
- **Benefits:**
  - Insights inform later sessions
  - Spread workload
  - Identify saturation point
- **How:**
  - Review session summaries regularly
  - Maintain research journal
  - Note emerging patterns
  - Adjust guide based on learnings
- **Formal coding:** Wait until all data collected to avoid biasing toward early sessions

**Q: How many codes is too many?**

A:
- **No fixed number,** but guidelines:
  - 30-100 codes typical for 8-10 session study
  - <30 codes: May be too broad; missing nuance
  - >100 codes: Likely too granular; will be hard to synthesize
- **Solution if too many:**
  - Group similar codes
  - Hierarchical structure (parent/child codes)
  - Merge redundant codes
- **Code is too specific if:** It only applies to one participant or moment
- **Code is too broad if:** It applies to everything; not discriminating

**Q: What if team members disagree on interpretation?**

A:
- **This is normal and valuable!**
- **Process:**
  1. Articulate both interpretations clearly
  2. Return to data together
  3. Identify source of disagreement (factual vs. interpretive)
  4. Test both interpretations against full dataset
  5. Discuss which better fits evidence
  6. Document decision and rationale
- **Sometimes:** Both interpretations are valid; report both
- **Remember:** Disagreement improves rigor

**Q: How do I know when I've reached code/theme saturation?**

A:
- **Code saturation:** No new codes emerging from new data
- **Signs:**
  - New sessions confirm existing codes
  - Rare to add new code
  - Patterns becoming clearer, not more complex
- **Typically:** After 6-8 similar participants
- **Action:** Note when saturation reached; justifies stopping data collection

**Q: Should I use software or can I do it manually?**

A:
- **You can absolutely analyze manually** (spreadsheet + docs)
- **Use software when:**
  - Large dataset (>15 sessions)
  - Team coding (need collaboration features)
  - Want automation (code frequency, co-occurrence)
  - Academic rigor requirements
- **Don't need software for:**
  - Small studies (5-10 sessions)
  - Solo analysis
  - Straightforward questions
- **Most important:** Systematic process, not the tool

**Q: How do I avoid confirmation bias?**

A:
- **Before analysis:**
  - Document your expectations
  - Share assumptions with team
- **During analysis:**
  - Code systematically (all data, not highlights)
  - Actively search for disconfirming evidence
  - Count frequencies; don't rely on memory
  - Challenge conclusions that match expectations
- **After analysis:**
  - Peer review
  - Ask: "What would change my mind?"
  - Report contradictions honestly

**Q: What if most of the data is saying the same thing? Isn't that boring?**

A:
- **Consistency is a finding!** Not boring - validating.
- **Strong consensus tells you:**
  - This is reliable insight
  - Safe to act on
  - High-priority issue if negative
- **What to do:**
  - Report the consistency
  - Look for nuances in how it's expressed
  - Explore why consensus exists
  - Note any outliers and why they differ
- **Boring would be:** Not learning anything new; this is clear learning

**Q: Can I include findings from only 1-2 participants?**

A:
- **Yes, but carefully:**
  - Label as "emerging pattern" or "some participants"
  - Don't generalize: "Some users..." not "Users..."
  - Note low frequency
  - Consider why only few mentioned it
- **Include if:**
  - Particularly insightful edge case
  - Reveals important variation
  - Suggests future exploration area
- **Don't include if:**
  - Idiosyncratic to individual
  - Not relevant to research questions
  - Would distract from main findings

**Q: How detailed should my analysis documentation be?**

A:
- **Detailed enough that:**
  - Someone else could understand your process
  - You could defend findings months later
  - Team member could continue if you left
  - You meet audit/compliance needs (if applicable)
- **Minimum documentation:**
  - Analysis plan
  - Codebook (final version)
  - Thematic map
  - Evidence for each finding
  - Major decisions made
- **Nice to have:**
  - Full audit trail
  - Peer review notes
  - Alternative interpretations considered

**Q: What if the research doesn't answer all the research questions?**

A:
- **This happens!** Be honest about it.
- **Possible reasons:**
  - Question wasn't addressable with this method
  - Didn't probe deeply enough
  - Sample didn't include right participants
  - Question wasn't relevant to participants
- **What to do:**
  - Report what you did learn
  - Document which questions remain unanswered
  - Explain why (method? sample? design?)
  - Recommend approach for answering in future
- **Don't:** Fabricate findings or over-extend data to force answers
